The catalog: products & variants

Lesson 1 put items into a cart without asking what an item is. This lesson answers that. In BigCommerce the thing a shopper buys is not one flat object — it is a product, its variants, its options, and its modifiers, and the differences between those four decide what has a SKU, what tracks inventory, and what ends up on a shipping label.

The concept

In any store, the word "product" hides an ambiguity. A T-shirt is one product in the catalog and on the marketing page — but "small red shirt" and "large blue shirt" are different things in the warehouse, counted and restocked separately. And "add a monogram" is a third kind of thing: it changes the shirt the buyer receives without being a different shirt on the shelf.

So a catalog has to separate three ideas that casual speech blurs: the marketing entity (the product you browse to), the stock-keeping unit (the exact item picked, counted, and shipped), and the customization (a change to that item that the warehouse does not count). Get these mixed up and inventory numbers lie.

How BigCommerce handles it

BigCommerce models the catalog as "a store's collection of physical and digital products," where "products are the primary catalog entity."1 Under each product sit variants, the options that generate them, and modifiers. The four fit together like this:

          PRODUCT  "T-Shirt"   ← the catalog entity (name, price, type)
          ┌──────────────┴───────────────┐
          │                              │
   VARIANT OPTIONS                   MODIFIERS
   the facets that build SKUs        change fulfillment, NOT the SKU
   ┌──────────┬──────────┐          ┌─────────────────────────┐
   Size: S M L  Color: R G B         "Monogram text: _______"
          │                          "☑ add gift wrap (+$5)"
          │ combinations auto-generate       │
          ▼                                  │ no new SKU
   VARIANTS (each has a SKU)                 │ no inventory tracked
   ┌────────────┬────────────┐               ▼
   S/Red  →  SKU TS-S-R      the same variant is picked off the
   L/Blue →  SKU TS-L-B      shelf, then customized before shipping
   … one per combination
   (tracked, counted, restocked)

Variant options build SKUs down the left. Modifiers hang off the product and never touch the SKU. That split is the whole lesson.

① The product — the catalog entity

The product is the top-level thing: "the primary function of the ecommerce platform is to sell products."1 A minimal product carries a name, a price, categories, and a type — either physical ("exist in a physical form, have a weight, and are sold by merchants to ship to customers") or digital ("non-physical products, including downloadable files… and services").1 Physical products carry a weight; digital ones do not. That type field matters downstream — remember from Lesson 1 that only physical products force a consignment and a shipping option.

② Variants — the things on the shelf

A variant is the actual saleable unit: "an item as it sits on the shelf in the warehouse or a particular saleable product."1 Each variant "must have a SKU code" and can carry its own price, weight, and image, or inherit them from the product.1 Variants are what inventory is counted against — "I care when I run out of small, red shirts — I need to re-stock!"2 A product with no options is not special-cased away: "the product is its own variant — called a base variant."1 So every product has at least one variant, always.

③ Variant options — the facets that generate variants

Variant options are the choices that build variants: "a product might have the variant options size and color," each with values (small/medium/large; red/green/blue).2 Their combinations become variants — and in the control panel, options "will automatically generate variants when created."1 Two sizes × three colors gives six variants, each its own SKU. The option is the question ("what size?"); the variant is a specific answered combination with stock behind it.

④ Modifiers — customizations that don't move the SKU

A modifier is "any choice a shopper can make that doesn't affect the variant that's selected"2 — a checkbox for shipping insurance, or text to engrave. It can add cost or capture input, but "the modifier will not change the SKU/variant fulfilled, and you cannot track inventory against combinations of modifier values."1 Same item off the shelf; something is just done to it before it ships. That is the line every catalog decision turns on: does this choice pick a different item, or change what happens to the same item?

Where it surfaces in the API

The catalog lives in the REST Management API (the v3 Catalog), and the four ideas above are four distinct resources — which, as with the pipeline objects in Lesson 1, is the clearest sign they are genuinely different things.

  • Products — /catalog/products. The base entity: name, price, type, weight, categories.1
  • Variants — /catalog/products/{id}/variants. Each with its own sku, and optional price/weight/image overrides.3
  • Variant options — /catalog/products/{id}/options. The facets (size, color) and their values.3
  • Modifiers — /catalog/products/{id}/modifiers. The customizations that don't produce SKUs.3

The three catalog objects, scrubbed to the fields that carry the distinction. Note the v3 data / meta envelope wrapping each response:

// PRODUCT — /v3/catalog/products/{id}
{
  "data": {
    "id": 12345,
    "name": "Premium Headphones",
    "type": "physical",              // or "digital" — decides weight + shipping
    "sku": "HEADPHONES-001",
    "price": "99.99",
    "weight": "0.5",                 // physical only
    "inventory_tracking": "product", // "none" | "product" | "variant"
    "base_variant_id": 54321,        // even a plain product has one variant
    "categories": [ 15, 23 ]
  },
  "meta": {}
}

The product: the catalog entity. Note base_variant_id — the model never lets a product exist without a variant.

// VARIANT — /v3/catalog/products/{id}/variants/{variant_id}
{
  "data": {
    "id": 54322,
    "product_id": 12345,
    "sku": "HEADPHONES-001-RED",     // its own SKU
    "price": 109.99,                 // overrides product price, or null to inherit
    "inventory_level": 45,           // ← stock is counted HERE, per variant
    "option_values": [               // which option choices this variant answers
      { "option_id": 101, "id": 201, "label": "Red" },
      { "option_id": 102, "id": 205, "label": "Over-ear" }
    ]
  }
}

A variant carries the SKU and the inventory_level. option_values pin it to a specific combination.

// MODIFIER — /v3/catalog/products/{id}/modifiers/{modifier_id}
{
  "data": {
    "id": 88,
    "product_id": 12345,
    "type": "text",                  // text | checkbox | dropdown | …
    "display_name": "Engraving",
    "required": false,
    "option_values": [
      { "id": 301, "label": "Add insurance",
        "adjusters": { "price":  { "type": "relative", "amount": 5.00 },
                       "weight": { "type": "relative", "amount": 0.10 } } }
    ]
    // ← no sku, no inventory_level anywhere. Only adjusters.
  }
}

A modifier can adjust price and weight, but it has no SKU and no inventory field — the tell that it never becomes a distinct stock item.

One practical wrinkle worth carrying: this is the v3 catalog. BigCommerce has a legacy v2 catalog with a different, flatter shape, and the two are not identical — when reading old integrations or docs, check which version you are in before trusting a field name.1

Why the split matters

The four resources exist to keep three numbers honest: what a thing is called, how many of it are in stock, and what was done to it on the way out. Variants and their options own the stock question — every distinct combination is a SKU, so the warehouse can count "small red shirts" without the marketing product getting in the way. Modifiers own the customization question, deliberately kept off the inventory ledger because a monogram is not a different shirt to restock. And the base-variant rule means the model has no awkward special case: a plain product still has exactly one variant with one SKU, so everything downstream — pricing, line items, fulfillment — can assume "a purchase is a variant" and never branch on whether the product had options.

Key line "A product is the catalog entity; a variant is the SKU on the shelf; variant options are the facets whose combinations generate variants; a modifier changes fulfillment without changing the SKU. Variants track inventory, modifiers never do — and every product has at least one variant, because a plain product is its own base variant."
The trap Treating "options" as one idea. BigCommerce splits them: variant options generate new SKUs and are inventory-tracked; modifiers do not and cannot be. Ask "does this choice change which physical item is picked?" Size and color → variant option (small red shirt is a different SKU). Engraving text or a gift-wrap checkbox → modifier (same shirt, then customized). Model a size as a modifier and your stock counts silently become fiction — the warehouse never learns it ran out of larges.

Recall — answer out loud before peeking:

① A plain product has no size/color choices. How many variants does it have, and what is that called?

show answer

One — itself. It is its own base variant, with one SKU. Every product has at least one variant.

② Size and color each have three values. How many variants, and why do they each need a SKU?

show answer

Nine (3 × 3 combinations), auto-generated from the options. Each is a distinct item on the shelf, so each needs its own SKU to track inventory separately.

③ A shopper can type text to be engraved. Is that a variant option or a modifier, and what is the consequence for inventory?

show answer

A modifier. It changes fulfillment, not the SKU, so no inventory is tracked against it — the same variant is picked and then engraved.

④ Which product type forces a weight and, recalling Lesson 1, a shipping option at checkout?

show answer

physical. Physical products have weight and must ship, so they force a consignment with a selected_shipping_option. Digital products do not.
