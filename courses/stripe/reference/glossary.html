How Stripe Works · Reference

Glossary

The shared vocabulary for this course, in plain language, no finance background assumed. Every lesson uses these terms exactly as defined here. Print it; keep it next to you.

The parties

TermDefinition
CardholderThe person who owns the card and is paying. (Lesson 1)
MerchantCard-industry word for the business being paid. Nothing more to it. (Lesson 1)
Issuer / issuing bankThe customer's bank: the one that issued (gave out) the card in their wallet. Holds the cardholder's money or credit line, and makes the approve/decline decision on every payment. Memory hook: the issuer issued the card. (Lesson 1)
Acquirer / acquiring bankThe business's bank in the card system. Old jargon: it "acquires" the transaction from the merchant — takes the payment claim, routes it through the networks, and receives the money on the business's behalf. Memory hook: the acquirer acquires the money for the seller. Stripe plays this role. (Lesson 1)
Card networkVisa, Mastercard, American Express, Discover. Not banks — they hold nobody's money. Shared rails plus rulebook that connect thousands of acquirers to thousands of issuers, so no bank needs a direct line to any other. Also called a "card scheme". (Lesson 1)
GatewayThe software front door: encrypts card details at checkout and carries them safely into the banking chain. (Lesson 1)
ProcessorThe message relay: shuttles the authorization request and the answer between merchant, acquirer, and network. Stripe is gateway + processor + acquirer in one provider. (Lesson 1)

The money's states

TermDefinition
AuthorizationThe issuer's "yes" to a payment, plus a reservation of the amount. Messages only; no money moves. Runs in seconds. (Lesson 1)
HoldThe reservation itself: the amount is fenced off on the cardholder's account, not taken. Same mechanism as a hotel deposit hold at check-in. Expires if never captured (7 days for most online card payments). (Lesson 1)
CaptureThe merchant's instruction to take the held amount for real. Default in Stripe: immediate on confirmation. Can be deferred (authorize now, capture at shipping time); capturing less than authorized releases the rest. (Lesson 1)
SettlementThe actual movement of money between banks: issuer → network → acquirer, batched, days after capture. Lands in the merchant's Stripe balance. (Lesson 1)
Stripe balanceThe merchant's money held at Stripe before it reaches their bank account. Two portions: pending (settled but not yet released) and available (ready to pay out). (Lesson 1)
Settlement timing (T+X)How long captured funds stay pending before becoming available, counted from transaction day T. US default: T+2 business days. (Lesson 1)
PayoutStripe depositing available balance into the merchant's regular bank account. (Lesson 1)
Payout scheduleHow often payouts happen: daily, weekly, monthly, or manual. Independent dial from settlement timing — timing controls when funds become available, schedule controls how often available funds are swept out. (Lesson 1)

The fees

TermDefinition
InterchangeThe slice of the per-transaction fee kept by the issuer, as payment for carrying the credit and fraud risk and running the cardholder's account. (Lesson 1)
Scheme feesThe slice kept by the card network ("scheme" = industry word for network), as payment for the routing, plus extras like cross-border charges. (Lesson 1)

The API machinery

TermDefinition
PaymentIntentStripe's state-machine object tracking one payment from creation to completion. One per cart or checkout session, surviving failed attempts. Its status field is always one of the lifecycle states. (Lesson 2)
ChargeOne payment attempt. A PaymentIntent accumulates a Charge per attempt, so three declines and a success are four Charges under one intent. (Lesson 2)
Client secretA unique key returned when a PaymentIntent is created. Lets the customer's browser read the intent's safe fields and drive it forward (confirm, complete 3DS) without exposing sensitive data or the merchant's API key. (Lesson 2)
ConfirmationThe action that fires a PaymentIntent: starts the actual authorization toward the issuer. Usually done from the browser with the client secret. (Lesson 2)
3D Secure (3DS)The card industry's protocol for verifying the payer is the real cardholder mid-payment: one-time codes, banking-app approval, biometrics. Branded Visa Secure / Mastercard Identity Check / Amex SafeKey. (Lesson 3)
SCAStrong Customer Authentication: regulation (PSD2 in the EEA, with UK/India/Japan/Australia equivalents) that can make authentication legally required for card payments. (Lesson 3)
ACSAccess Control Server: the issuer-side system that owns the 3DS decision and picks frictionless vs challenge. (Lesson 3)
Frictionless / challengeThe two 3DS paths. Frictionless: the bank authenticates from background data, customer sees nothing. Challenge: the customer actively proves identity. The issuer chooses. (Lesson 3)
Liability shiftAfter successful 3DS authentication, liability for fraud disputes moves from merchant to issuer. Has carve-outs; non-fraud disputes unaffected. (Lesson 3)
WebhookStripe calling the merchant: an HTTPS POST carrying an Event object to a URL the merchant registered. The only channel for outcomes that happen after the API request ends. (Lesson 4)
Event objectThe JSON payload of a webhook: a type naming what happened, a data.object snapshot, and a unique id used for deduplication. (Lesson 4)
Signing secretPer-endpoint shared secret used to HMAC-sign webhook deliveries (the Stripe-Signature header). Verifying it is what authenticates a webhook as genuinely from Stripe. (Lesson 4)
IdempotentAn operation is idempotent when doing it twice has the same effect as doing it once. (Lesson 5)
Idempotency keyClient-generated unique string on a POST request. Stripe stores the first response under it (~24h) and replays it for retries, so a retry asks "what happened?" instead of acting again. (Lesson 5)
Balance transactionOne ledger entry in the Stripe balance: a credit or debit, net of fees, carrying its own available_on timestamp. The balance is the sum of these entries. (Lesson 6)
available_onThe per-entry timestamp at which a pending balance transaction becomes available, set from the payment method's and country's settlement timing. (Lesson 6)
Reserve balanceFunds Stripe sets aside from a merchant's balance to cover expected refunds or disputes. (Lesson 6)
Top-upThe merchant pushing external money into a Stripe balance (e.g. to fund refunds when the balance is short). (Lesson 6)

Connect

TermDefinition
PlatformThe business running a marketplace or software product whose users get paid through it. Creates and manages connected accounts. (Lesson 7)
Connected accountA full Stripe account (own balance, payouts, verification) representing one seller on a platform. Created via the platform, not by the seller at stripe.com. (Lesson 7)
Controller propertiesThe modern Connect model: per-account dials assigning each responsibility (onboarding, dashboard, fee collection, losses) to platform or Stripe independently. (Lesson 7)
Standard / Express / CustomLegacy account types: preset bundles of the controller dials, fixed at account creation. Standard = seller self-serve with full Dashboard; Express = slim dashboard, platform bears risk; Custom = fully white-label, platform does and owns everything. (Lesson 7)
Direct chargeCharge created on the connected account: the seller is the merchant, funds land in the seller's balance, refunds debit the seller. Platform earns an application fee. (Lesson 8)
Destination chargeCharge created on the platform, with a portion forwarded to one connected account in the same operation (transfer_data). Platform pays fees and holds refund/dispute liability. (Lesson 8)
Separate charges and transfersCharge on the platform now; independent transfer(s) to one or more connected accounts later. Maximum flexibility; platform holds liability and must keep available balance ahead of its transfers. (Lesson 8)
Application feeThe platform's per-charge cut on a direct charge, credited to the platform's balance. (Lesson 8)
TransferA movement of funds from the platform's balance to a connected account's balance. The building block of destination charges and separate-charges flows. (Lesson 8)
