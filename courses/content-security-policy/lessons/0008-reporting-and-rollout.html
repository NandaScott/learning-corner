Content Security Policy · Lesson 8

Reporting & rollout

You can write a perfect strict policy (L7) and still take the site down by shipping it — because you don't actually know every inline script, handler, and third-party loader your app depends on. This final lesson is how CSP is operated: a report-only mode that breaks nothing, violation reports that tell you what would break, and the rollout that turns the two into a safe path to enforcement.

The concept

CSP has a built-in dry-run. Alongside the enforcing Content-Security-Policy header there's a Content-Security-Policy-Report-Only header that evaluates the exact same policy but blocks nothing — it only emits a violation report for anything that would have been blocked. That single mechanism is what lets you deploy a tight policy to production traffic and learn what it breaks before it breaks anything.1

Content-Security-Policy:             enforce — blocks + reports
Content-Security-Policy-Report-Only: observe — reports only, blocks NOTHING

both can be sent at once:  enforce a loose policy today,
                           report-only a stricter one you're testing

The report-only header is the safety valve. Serve it with your candidate strict policy, watch the reports roll in from real users, fix what's legitimate, then promote it to the enforcing header.

The steps

Step 1 — Report-Only: observe without blocking

Send your candidate policy in Content-Security-Policy-Report-Only. Every resource or inline block that violates it is allowed to proceed as normal, but the browser records a violation. Nothing your users do breaks; you simply accumulate evidence. This is step zero of any CSP deployment — never flip a new policy straight to enforce.1

Step 2 — The violation report: what the browser tells you

Each violation produces a JSON report and an in-page securitypolicyviolation DOM event. The report names precisely what happened:1

effectiveDirective  "script-src-elem"      which directive did the blocking
blockedURL          "inline" / an origin   what got blocked
disposition         "enforce" | "report-only"   WHICH header fired ← key field
documentURL         the page                originalPolicy  the full policy string
sourceFile / lineNumber / columnNumber      where in the page
sample              first chars of the offending inline code (if 'report-sample' set)

The disposition field is the one people miss: it tells you whether a report came from the enforcing policy or a report-only one — essential when you run both at once. sample only appears if the directive included 'report-sample' (L5).

Step 3 — report-uri vs report-to: two eras of the endpoint

Where reports go has two mechanisms, and they don't look alike:2

  • report-uri <url> (CSP1, deprecated) — POSTs directly to the URL as Content-Type: application/csp-report, wrapped as {"csp-report": { "document-uri":…, "violated-directive":…, "blocked-uri":… }} (hyphenated keys).
  • report-to <group> (CSP3) — names a group defined by a separate Reporting-Endpoints header, delivered through the generic Reporting API as application/reports+json with the camelCase fields above.
Edge — send both, and know which wins report-to is preferred but only reached broad browser support recently, so real deployments send both. The rule: browsers that support report-to ignore report-uri — so you won't get duplicate reports; older browsers fall back to report-uri. And report-to is inert without a matching Reporting-Endpoints: name="https://…" header defining the group.2

Step 4 — The rollout playbook

1. Report-Only the candidate policy      breaks nothing; collect real-traffic violations
2. Triage the reports                     separate YOUR gaps from noise (browser
                                          extensions inject junk you can't control)
3. Fix legitimately-blocked things        add a nonce, refactor an inline handler,
                                          hash a static block — don't just widen the policy
4. Flip to enforcing                      move the cleaned policy to Content-Security-Policy
5. Keep a Report-Only for the NEXT tightening   enforce today's, observe tomorrow's

Step 2 is where deployments stall: report-only endpoints get flooded with violations from browser extensions and injected content you don't own. Triage by sourceFile/blockedURL; don't loosen your policy to silence noise you can't fix.

Why this matters

1. Reporting is what makes CSP deployable at all. Without report-only, a strict policy is a guess you can only test in production by breaking it. With it, CSP becomes an evidence-driven rollout: observe, fix, enforce. The strict policy of L7 is only realistic because this mechanism exists.

2. disposition and "which header" are the operator's tells. Running enforce + report-only together is standard practice — a loose enforced policy protecting today while a strict report-only one is validated for tomorrow. Reading disposition to know which fired, and knowing report-to supersedes report-uri where supported, is the difference between drowning in reports and driving a rollout.

3. The trap is loosening to silence noise. The instinct when reports flood in is to widen the policy until they stop. That quietly rebuilds the permissive allowlist L6 warned about. The discipline is to fix your violations (nonce it, refactor it, hash it) and filter the rest — keep the policy tight, keep the noise out of scope.

Key line "You roll CSP out with Content-Security-Policy-Report-Only — same policy, blocks nothing, just emits violation reports — then fix what's legitimately blocked and promote it to the enforcing header. Reports carry effectiveDirective, blockedURL, and a disposition of enforce vs report-only. Endpoints come in two forms: deprecated report-uri (direct POST) and report-to (via the Reporting API and a Reporting-Endpoints header); browsers that support report-to ignore report-uri, so you send both."
The trap Shallow: "set report-uri and read the logs." Deeper: report-uri is deprecated and ignored by browsers that understand report-to; you send both during the transition; and the real work is triage — separating your fixable gaps from un-ownable extension noise — done via report-only before you ever enforce. Reciting the headers without the rollout discipline is the giveaway.

Recall — answer out loud before peeking:

① What does Content-Security-Policy-Report-Only do differently from the enforcing header?

show answer

It evaluates the same policy but blocks nothing — it only emits violation reports for what would have been blocked. It's the dry-run you deploy before enforcing.

② Both report-uri and report-to are set. A modern browser sends reports where?

show answer

To report-to (via the Reporting API) — browsers that support report-to ignore report-uri. Older browsers use report-uri. That's why you serve both.

③ Which report field tells you whether a violation came from the enforcing or the report-only policy?

show answer

disposition — either "enforce" or "report-only". Essential when both headers are live at once.

④ Reports flood in from a browser extension injecting script. Correct response?

show answer

Filter/triage it out — it's un-ownable noise. Do not loosen the policy to silence it; that rebuilds the permissive allowlist. Only fix violations from your own code (nonce, hash, refactor).
