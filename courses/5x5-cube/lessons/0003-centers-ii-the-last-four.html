5x5 Cube · Lesson 3

Centers II: the last four

Two centers are done. The remaining four wrap around them in a ring, and the move that builds them is the one you already know — lift, park, restore. What changes is the room you have to work in. Every center you finish takes a face out of play, and by the last two the face you would scribble on is the face you are trying to solve.

The concept

Hold the two finished centers (white and yellow) on the left and right.1 The four faces still open — top, front, bottom, back — form a ring around that left–right axis. Filling them uses the same three-bar build and the same lift–park–restore round trip from Lesson 2. There is no new algorithm to learn in this lesson.1

The difficulty is spatial, not algorithmic. In Lesson 2 one face needed protecting (white, parked on the bottom). Now two centers are held on the sides, and both are adjacent to every face in the ring — so a careless wide turn can break either of them. As more centers go in, the number of faces you can turn freely keeps shrinking.1 The last two centers are the pinch: no free face is left to assemble a bar on.

The steps

START  white + yellow held on LEFT / RIGHT
       four open faces form a ring:   U  F  D  B
   │
1. adjacency   pick centers 3 & 4 as two ADJACENT faces (e.g. U, then F)
2. build 3     middle bar + 2 outer bars — round-trip each insert to
   │           protect the held L/R centers
3. build 4     same build, on a face next to #3
   │           (now L, R and #3 are all off-limits)
   ▼
THE LAST TWO   the two open faces left are the last two centers —
4. build 5     no free face to scratch-build on; stage the bar in the
   │           space you have and round-trip it in
5. build 6     the final pieces fall in as 5 completes;
               awkward positions use the same engine

One engine throughout. Steps 2–5 are all lift–park–restore; the only thing that changes down the list is how much free space you have to stage a bar.

Hold the finished pair on left and right

Turn the whole cube so white and yellow sit on the left and right faces.1 That leaves top, front, bottom, and back open — a four-face ring circling the left–right axis. Every remaining bar gets built and inserted somewhere in that ring, and every insert has to leave the two held centers intact.

Centers 3 and 4 must be adjacent, not opposite

Choose your third and fourth centers as two faces that touch each other — say the top, then the front — never two opposite faces.1 The reason is what it leaves behind. The ring is a cycle (U–F–D–B and back to U), so taking an adjacent pair always leaves the other two faces adjacent as well. Solve top and front, and the last two are bottom and back — still next to each other, which is the workable shape for the endgame. Solve two opposite faces instead and the last two would sit across the cube from one another, with no shared space to build in.

Take an adjacent pair for centers 3 and 4 and the leftover pair is adjacent too. That is what keeps the last two centers solvable.

The round trip now protects two centers

The insert is the Lesson 2 maneuver, unchanged: build an outer bar on a free face, lift it with a wide turn, park it out of the return path with a U2 (or D2), then restore. The difference is what a wide turn now costs. A move that grabs the right layers disturbs the right-held center; one that grabs the left layers disturbs the left. So you keep every insert to a clean round trip — the wide turn and its inverse cancel everywhere except where the parking spin moved things.1 With two centers held instead of one, the set of faces you can turn without cost is smaller, and it shrinks again with center 3, and again with center 4.

Watch the same engine on the player. The cube wears the L2C view: the two adjacent open centers (top and front) show in full color, and every already-built center is dimmed — including the pair held on the sides.2 This is exactly the endgame geometry, two adjacent faces left to fill. The stickering follows pieces, so a bar you stage on the front stays bright as it rides up on the lift.

Press ⏮ then ▶. The bright faces are the two adjacent centers still being filled; every dimmed center is already built and must survive the insert. The front bar lifts on Rw, the top parks it on U2, and Rw' restores what the lift displaced. Same round trip as Lesson 2 — now it is guarding more.

The last two centers: no free face

When only two faces are left open, there is no spare face to freely assemble a bar on — both open faces are centers you are trying to finish.1 You still build with inner bar then two outer bars,3 but you stage each bar in the narrow space that remains and round-trip it into place with the same lift–park–restore move. Most positions fall in cleanly; a few land awkwardly. Those hard cases are what the primary-source video covers from 3:03.1 The mission is reliability, not a memorized case list — so when a piece traps itself, note the exact position and bring it to the teacher, and lean on the round trip you already trust rather than hunting for a special algorithm.

Why this matters

The whole centers stage is one move applied under shrinking freedom. Lesson 2 taught lift–park–restore with a single protected face and a whole cube of scratch space. This lesson is the same move with two protected centers and a work area that vanishes to nothing by the sixth center. Recognizing that keeps the endgame calm: when the last two feel hard, it is the tight space talking, not a gap in your technique.

The adjacency rule is the one piece of foresight that pays off here. Choosing centers 3 and 4 next to each other guarantees the final two are a matched adjacent pair, which is the only configuration the round trip can still reach once the cube is nearly full. Solve them opposite and you strand yourself.

Key line "Same round trip, less room. Two centers held on the sides, a shrinking ring to fill — and by the last two, the face you would scribble on is the face you're solving."
The trap The shallow expectation is that the last centers need special algorithms. They don't — it is lift–park–restore the whole way down. What actually changes is the free space available to stage a bar, which is why the last two feel hard. The one decision that matters is made early: build centers 3 and 4 adjacent, so the final two are an adjacent pair you can still work. Naming the tight workspace as the difficulty — rather than reaching for a memorized case — is the deep answer.

Recall — answer out loud before peeking:

① Where do the two finished centers go while you build the remaining four?

show answer

On the left and right faces. That leaves top, front, bottom, and back — a four-face ring around the left–right axis — open to build in.

② Why must centers 3 and 4 be two adjacent faces, not opposite ones?

show answer

The ring is a cycle, so taking an adjacent pair leaves the other two faces adjacent as well. That keeps the last two centers next to each other — the workable shape. An opposite pair would strand the final two across the cube with no shared space to build in.

③ Is there a new algorithm for the last four centers?

show answer

No. It is the Lesson 2 lift–park–restore round trip (Rw U2 Rw' and its variants) throughout. Only the free space to stage a bar changes.

④ Why are the last two centers the hardest part of the stage?

show answer

No free face is left — both open faces are centers you are finishing, so there is no spare face to assemble a bar on. The difficulty is the tight workspace, not a new move.

⑤ What makes the move set "more restricted" as you finish more centers?

show answer

Each finished center is a face you can no longer turn freely without breaking it. Two held on the sides, then a third, then a fourth — every one removes safe turns, so more inserts must be clean round trips.
