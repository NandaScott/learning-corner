5x5 Cube · Lesson 2

Centers I: the first two

Stage 1 begins. The first center costs nothing — every move is legal because nothing is built yet. The second center introduces the move pattern that carries the rest of the stage: put a bar in place, then pay the round trip that protects what's already done.

The concept

A center is the inner 3×3 block of one face, all one color, matching its fixed middle piece (Lesson 1). You build it as three 1×3 bars: a middle bar through the fixed center, then two outer bars attached to its sides.1

The order is white first, then yellow.1 Not because those colors are special — because they're opposite. While you build yellow, white sits on the bottom face, out of the way of everything you do on top. The real subject of this lesson is the difference between the two builds: the white center is unconstrained, and the yellow center is the first time a move can cost you finished work.

The steps

FIRST CENTER (white)                 SECOND CENTER (yellow)
1. middle bar    + F +               4. turn white to the BOTTOM
2. outer bar     x + x  → attach        (yellow fixed center is now on top)
3. outer bar     x + x  → attach     5. middle yellow bar, built on the top face
                                     6. outer bar on the front → Rw U2 Rw'
                                     7. repeat step 6 for the last bar

Steps 1–3 are a free build. Steps 6–7 use the lift–park–restore maneuver this lesson teaches.

The anatomy of one center, as bars

One center block, split into bars: the middle bar is + F + (the fixed center and two plus-centers); each outer bar is x + x (two x-centers around a plus-center).1 Knowing which piece types a bar needs tells you what to hunt for.

Building a bar

Join two pieces of the color first, then bring in the third. At this stage the moves are intuitive, not memorized: turn faces to sit two pieces side by side, then rotate the pair next to the third. If a piece is on the wrong face, a wide turn (Rw, Lw) travels it across the cube. Expect this to feel like puzzle-solving rather than algorithm-following — that's the correct experience for centers.1

The white face mid-build: middle bar done (top), then one outer bar attached (bottom). One bar to go.

The first center is free

While white is the only thing being built, no move can break anything. Use that freedom: turn any face, slice any layer, experiment. The only goal is the 3×3 white block around the fixed white center. This is also where bar-building intuition gets trained cheaply — mistakes cost nothing.

The second center: the constraint arrives

Turn the whole cube so white faces the bottom.1 Because the six fixed centers never move relative to each other (Lesson 1), the fixed center now on top is automatically yellow. Build the yellow middle bar directly on the top face — pieces can be joined up there with U turns and wide turns that stay off the bottom.

The outer bars are the new skill. Build each one vertically on the front face, then run the maneuver: 

Rw    lift     the front bar rides up onto the top face —
               the same turn pulls a column OUT of white
U2    park     spin the top: the new bar leaves the return path
Rw'   restore  the white column slides home; yellow keeps its bar

Lift–park–restore. Rw and Rw' cancel everywhere except where the U2 moved things in between.1

Watch it on the player below. The cube wears the opposite-centers view, a training stickering that keeps only the center pieces of the top and bottom faces — exactly the pair this maneuver is about, the center being built and the center being protected. Every other piece is blanked, edges and the other four centers included.2 The stickering follows pieces, so the new bar stays bright while it waits on the front face: it is made of top-face center pieces wherever it happens to sit. Because the view hides every edge, it stops helping once the centers stage is over — but for centers it removes exactly the pieces you shouldn't be looking at.

Press ⏮ then ▶. Only the two opposite centers' pieces are visible. Watch the front-right column ride up on Rw, the top spin on U2, and the displaced column drop back home on Rw'. (The player's solved orientation has white on top rather than the bottom — the motion is identical to your grip.)

Why U2 and not U? Anything even — U, U', U2 — that moves the parked bar off the column you're about to slice back down. U2 is the habit worth building because it never collides with the middle bar you placed first.

Why this matters

The maneuver is the general engine, not a one-off trick. Its shape — do a move that breaks something, hide your progress from the undo, then undo the damage — is how every remaining center gets built, and the costs only rise from here: with white and yellow both done, more of the cube becomes off-limits and the free moves keep shrinking. Learning it now, when only one face needs protecting, is the cheap version.

It also explains the ordering rule from the map: white and yellow are chosen because they're opposite. The protected face spends the whole second build on the bottom, touched only by the brief Rw–Rw' round trips.

Key line "The first center is free. Every bar after that is bought with a round trip: lift the bar up, park it with U2, slice back to restore what the lift broke."
The trap The shallow version is memorizing Rw U2 Rw' as a magic insert. The deep version names each move's job: Rw both places the bar and damages the bottom center; U2 exists only to move the placed bar off the return path; Rw' is a pure undo of the damage. Once you can say that, you can improvise the maneuver from any angle instead of resetting to a memorized grip.

Recall — answer out loud before peeking:

① Which pieces make up the middle bar of a center? The outer bars?

show answer

Middle bar: plus-center, fixed center, plus-center (+ F +). Outer bars: x-center, plus-center, x-center (x + x).

② Which two centers come first, and where does the first one sit while you build the second?

show answer

White first, then yellow — opposites. White goes to the bottom face while yellow is built on top, keeping it out of the way.

③ In Rw U2 Rw', what is the U2 for?

show answer

It moves the just-placed yellow bar off the column that Rw' will slice back down. Without it, Rw' would undo the insertion along with the damage.

④ Why is the first center "free"?

show answer

Nothing else is built yet, so no move can destroy finished work. Any face or slice can be turned; the only goal is assembling the white block.

⑤ What does Rw do to the bottom center during an insertion, and what undoes it?

show answer

It pulls a column of the white center up out of the bottom face. Rw' — after the U2 — slices that column straight back home.
