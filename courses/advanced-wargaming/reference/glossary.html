Glossary

The canonical vocabulary of the advanced course. Every lesson uses these words in exactly these senses. For the five foundational principles (value, exchange rate, threat range, concentration, commitment) this course assumes and links to the Wargaming Fundamentals glossary rather than restating them. Grouped by the lesson that owns each term; grows as the arc is built.

Tempo & initiative (Lesson 1)

TermMeaning
InitiativeA state, not a die roll: the player with initiative sets the problems and the opponent only answers them. Distinct from the scoreboard — you can hold the initiative while behind on VP, or have lost it while ahead.
TempoInitiative held over time — turn after turn where the opponent spends their effort reacting to you and never runs their own plan. The advanced sense builds on the fundamentals' Tempo (the cost of deferral); here it is the wider game-long control of rhythm.
ActivationA unit doing its job once in a turn (a move, a volley, a charge). A resource, like a command point or a wound — and therefore something that can be spent, wasted, or denied.
Action economyTreating each activation as a countable resource across the turn. The lens under which removing a unit before it acts is not merely a kill but the deletion of a resource.
Double swingRemoving an enemy unit before it activates: you gain your unit's output and cancel theirs in one action. The sharpest form of the economy of exchanges — a trade the opponent never gets to trade back. Most literal in the Fight phase (the one phase that alternates activations).
Forcing reactionsPresenting threats the opponent cannot ignore — aimed at their scoring, not just their models — so their turn is consumed by defence. A turn spent answering you is a turn taken from their plan, with no model removed.
Alpha strikeBuying the initiative outright: go first, concentrate, and remove a piece so central the opponent spends the game recovering. High variance — size it as a target-band margin problem and weight the downside by the stakes.
Bait-and-punishBorrowing the initiative: offer an opening, let the opponent overextend to take it, and punish the overreach next turn. Gives up the first move for a stronger second one.

Target priority (Lesson 2)

TermMeaning
Target priorityThe discipline of identifying the one enemy piece whose removal most collapses their plan. Ties Principle 1 (value = what moves the score) to Principle 4 (concentrate on that one thing).
Dangerous vs decisiveDangerous = what a unit can do to you (its threat/output). Decisive = what its removal does to them (plan-collapse). The most violent target and the most valuable one are usually different pieces.
Linchpin / force multiplierA buff character, aura, or leader ability that makes several other units work. Its removal cliffs the whole cluster — leverage — which is why it usually beats the biggest gun as a target.
Create the shotThe 40K wrinkle: a Leader attached to a Bodyguard unit can't be picked out, so removing the linchpin means grinding through the unit or catching the character exposed. Target priority includes engineering the opportunity, not just naming the target.

Deception & threat overload (Lesson 3)

TermMeaning
Bait / feintOffering a target you want the opponent to take, so their most attractive move is the one that helps you. Exploits their tendency to take the move they can most easily justify.
Sacrificial piece / chaffA cheap unit whose job is to die profitably — screen, block, or eat an activation. Principle 2's winning-trade-where-models-die, made proactive: the loss is planned. Wins its trade when the tempo/space/activation bought exceeds its points.
Threat overloadPresenting more credible (can't-ignore) threats in one turn than the opponent has answers for, so one gets through no matter how they prioritise. Not dilution — several concentrated threats, each individually decisive.
Overload as inverted capability-mappingCapability-mapping (defence) covers the opponent's best move; overload (offence) stops them covering yours. Same map, opposite direction.

The hammer and anvil (Lesson 4)

TermMeaning
AnvilThe role that fixes the enemy — holds, pins, or presents a problem they can't ignore or leave — removing their freedom to reposition. Sun Tzu's direct force (zheng).
HammerThe mobile, high-output striking role that lands the decisive blow from a direction the enemy can't defend. Sun Tzu's indirect force (qi). A role, not a datasheet.
Four marks of a hammerHigh removal output, mobility (chooses the strike), durability (arrives intact), high cost (losing it early is catastrophic — hence hide it, Principle 5).
Reinterpreted anvilAgainst a shooting army you can't melee-pin, the anvil becomes objective pressure (make staying back lose them the game, P1) + space denial (take away room to kite, P3). Same job, positional tool.

Deployment & the first turn (Lesson 5)

TermMeaning
Win first (made physical)Deployment is where Principle 1's "win first, then fight" happens on the table — the positioning that decides the game is secured before combat.
First-turn choiceTake the first turn to alpha-strike, or give it to play reactively and punish. Army-dependent, not free value; the game's first tempo decision. In 40K the mission sets who goes first.
Expose vs holdDeployment as threat-range management: expose the anvil and chaff; hold the hammer out of the opponent's first-turn range or behind blockers.
Screening / zoningScreening = cheap units denying space (enemy can't arrive within 9" or charge through). Zoning = shaping where the enemy will go by what you threaten.
Reserves as optionalityUnits held off-table (Principle 5 physicalised): can't be shot turn one, committed after seeing the opponent's move, and they strip targets out of the enemy's first turn.

List construction (Lesson 6)

TermMeaning
List as thesisAn army read as a coherent plan to score, not a pile of strong datasheets. The test: what does it actually do to move the score-difference?
Threat spectrumThe ability to remove every target type (anti-tank, anti-horde, anti-elite). A gap hands the opponent a free plan. Concentration (Principle 4) as a build constraint.
Single point of failureA list that folds when one linchpin dies — target priority (Lesson 2) turned on yourself. Loses to a competent opponent's target priority.
Reading their listCapability-mapping run before deployment: their list is their thesis, telling you what to deny and what to target before a model is placed.

Risk & probability (Lesson 7)

TermMeaning
Average fails half the time"On average I kill it" means roughly half the time — the average is the centre of a distribution, not a floor. Plan on the range, not the average.
Robust vs reliantRobust: the plan wins across the likely range, including below-average rolls. Reliant: it wins only if the dice cooperate. Robust-to-bad-luck is the winning posture.
MarginCommitting enough that a below-average roll still clears the bar — the floor of the target band (Principle 4), scaled to the stakes.
Certainty placementGuaranteed-effect tools (re-rolls, auto-hits, mortal wounds, CP) are worth most on low-volume, high-stakes rolls, where variance is highest; wasted on high-volume rolls the average already protects.

Phase-craft (Lesson 8)

TermMeaning
Movement primacyThe movement phase executes most of threat range (Principle 3) with no dice; it decides which fights happen and on what terms. The least flashy phase and the decisive one.
Coherency40K 11th ed: every model within 2" horizontally of at least one other model in the unit, and within 9" horizontally of every other. The 2" of slack is the lever for spread (blunt blast, stretch footprint) vs bunch (hold coherency, hide behind a blocker).
Charge / pile-in / consolidate geometryCharge = 2D6, ending within engagement range (2" horizontal). Pile-in (before) and consolidate (after) = up to 3". The geometry decides who's in the fight and what you control when it ends.
Denial bubbleA screen where every point of a zone is within 9" of a screen model leaves no legal arrival for reserves/deep strike. Drawn model by model, in inches.
