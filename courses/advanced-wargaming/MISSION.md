# Mission

**Master the second tier of wargaming theory — tempo, target priority, deception, the hammer and anvil, deployment, list construction, felt probability, and phase-craft — deeply enough to run each cold and reason about fresh board states, built on the five foundational principles, with Warhammer 40,000 (11th edition) as the concrete instantiation and Sun Tzu's *Art of War* as the framing.**

## Why this mission

This course is the sequel to **Wargaming Fundamentals** (`courses/wargaming/`), which owns the five foundational principles — value, economy of exchanges, threat range, concentration of force, and commitment under uncertainty — plus the chain that orders them and the two cross-cutting skills. That course is complete and in its durability phase.

The five principles are the axioms. This course teaches the **operating theory built on top of them**: the recurring structures and decisions a strong player reasons about explicitly — tempo, which piece to remove, how to overload the opponent's answers, how to fix-and-strike, how the game is half-decided at deployment, how a list is a thesis about winning, how variance actually feels, and where theory meets the table in millimetres. Each is a *seed that expands into a full principle*, exactly as the five foundational ones did.

Same mould as the learner's other courses: the goal is **storage strength**, not fluency. Explain each advanced principle, the mechanism behind it, and the sharp edges — from memory, days later, unprompted. And explain each as *one of the five foundational principles firing in a specific structure*, so the advanced layer stays connected to the axioms rather than becoming a second flat list.

## The three framings, and how they relate

- **The theory** (the spine): eight second-tier principles, each grounded in one or more of the five foundational ones. This is what gets mastered.
- **Warhammer 40,000** (the instantiation, **11th edition**): the concrete game whose rules make the abstract principles measurable — I-go-you-go turns, activations, the alternating Fight phase, terrain objectives + Objective Control, force dispositions, reserves.
- **Sun Tzu's *Art of War*** (the framing): the 2,500-year-old statement that these are the structure of contest itself. Each lesson closes the loop back to a verified Giles-translation passage.

## Grounding constraints

- **Source-first, never parametric.** Sun Tzu quotes verified verbatim against the Lionel Giles translation. 40K mechanics verified against the current **11th-edition** core rules (official free GW download / Wahapedia `wh40k11ed` mirror) — teach the actual model (I-go-you-go turns, the alternating Fight phase, terrain objectives, OC, force dispositions), not folklore.
- **Tool-specific truth over generic theory.** The reference doc describes an "alternating-turn game" and "activations"; 40K is precisely **I-go-you-go** at the turn level, with **alternating activation only inside the Fight phase**. Flag that distinction explicitly wherever the generic frame and the 40K rule differ.
- **Build strictly forward.** Every lesson may lean on the five foundational principles (a completed prerequisite course) and on earlier lessons *in this course*, but never on later ones. Recall widgets answerable from the current lesson + all prior material only.

## Starting level

The learner completed Wargaming Fundamentals: owns the five principles, the chain, and the two cross-cutting skills; knows 40K rules; plays rarely. So this course assumes the five principles cold and does not re-derive them — it references them by name and shows each advanced principle as a specific firing of them.

## What "success" looks like

The learner can, unprompted:
1. Define **initiative** (who sets the problem) and **tempo** (holding it over time), and explain why removing a unit before it activates is a double swing. *(L1)*
2. Pick the **decisive** target over the merely dangerous one, and overload the opponent's answers. *(L2–L3)*
3. Explain the **hammer and anvil** as *zheng* (fix) + *qi* (strike), and why a hammer without an anvil chases shadows. *(L4)*
4. Read **deployment** and the first-turn choice as where "win first, then fight" physically happens. *(L5)*
5. Read a **list** as a thesis about how to win, and tune decisions to **variance** and **phase-craft**. *(L6–L8)*

## Notes for the teacher

- Keep every advanced principle nailed to one or more of the five foundational ones — teach the connection, not a parallel list.
- The reference doc's suggested order is load-bearing: tempo first (it deepens all five at once), then target priority + threat overload (they pair), then deployment, then list construction; risk/probability and phase-craft are woven through.
- Prioritise storage strength: recall widgets per lesson, spacing across sessions, an interleaved quiz once there's enough material.
