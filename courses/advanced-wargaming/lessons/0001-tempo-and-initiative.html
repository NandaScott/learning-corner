Advanced Wargaming Theory · Lesson 1

Tempo and initiative

This is the first advanced principle because it deepens all five foundational ones at once. The five principles told you what a move is worth and how to move the score-difference efficiently. Tempo is about who is steering while that happens, whether you are setting the problems the opponent has to solve, or solving theirs.

The concept

Initiative is a state, not a die roll: the player with initiative sets the problems, and the other player only ever answers them. Tempo is that initiative held over time, turn after turn where the opponent spends their effort reacting to you and never gets to run their own plan. The deepest form of control in a wargame is dictating the rhythm this way.

This sits directly on top of the fundamentals. It is Principle 1's "a lead is a race, not a bank balance" turned into an active weapon, and it is executed with threat range (Principle 3), concentration (Principle 4), and timing (Principle 5). It assumes you know those cold.

One rule fact sits under this lesson. Warhammer 40,000 is I-go-you-go: one player takes their entire turn: Command, Movement, Shooting, Charge, Fight. Only then does the opponent take theirs.1 Initiative is a state you manufacture inside that structure and then work to hold; the turn order gives you a sequence, not the initiative itself.

Steps 2–4 are the mechanism; step 1 is the state they produce. Tempo is that state, held across turns.

Step 1 — Initiative is about who reacts to whom

Every turn, one player is proposing and the other is disposing. The scoreboard measures who is winning; initiative measures who is steering. The two come apart: you can be behind on Victory Points and hold the initiative, forcing responses that are about to pay off, or be ahead and have lost it, scrambling to answer what the opponent now dictates. Holding the initiative across several turns in a row is what tempo names.

Step 2 — Action economy: an activation is a resource

Each unit does its job once per turn, a move, a volley, a charge. That single activation is a resource, exactly like a command point or a wound. If you remove an enemy unit before it activates, you have not only denied its damage but deleted an entire activation from the opponent's turn. You got your unit's output and cancelled theirs in one action. That is a double swing, and it is the sharpest form of the economy of exchanges: the best trade is one the opponent never gets to trade back.

  Their unit alive into their turn:  [MOVE][SHOOT][CHARGE][FIGHT]  full output
  You remove it first:               ✗  never activates            zero output
                                     └─ the activation is deleted, not just damaged

Denying damage subtracts from their turn. Deleting the activation subtracts the whole unit's turn.

Where is this most literal in 40K? The Fight phase, the one place the game genuinely alternates activations, where players take turns selecting an eligible unit to fight.1 If your unit is selected first and wipes theirs, that unit is never picked and never swings back: its activation is gone. In the other phases the I-go-you-go structure does the same thing more coarsely: kill a unit on your turn and it produces nothing across their entire following turn, a full round of output denied.

Step 3 — Forcing reactions

The opponent has a plan: the moves that score them the mission. Every turn they spend answering your threats is a turn they do not spend on that plan. So initiative is manufactured by presenting problems they cannot ignore: a unit poised to take an objective they need, a strike aimed at a piece they cannot afford to lose, so that their turn is consumed by defence. This is threat range (Principle 3) pointed at their scoring, not merely at their models: the threat that forces a reaction is the one that costs them the game to ignore. When their whole turn goes to answering you, you have taken a turn away from their plan without removing a single model.

Step 4 — Seizing it: the alpha strike and bait-and-punish

There are two routes to taking the initiative, and choosing between them is itself a tempo decision.

The alpha strike buys the initiative outright: go first, concentrate (Principle 4), and remove a piece so central that the opponent spends the rest of the game recovering rather than executing. It is high variance: if the blow underperforms you have overcommitted a large part of your army and handed them the tempo instead. Size it as a margin problem (the target band from Principle 4) and weight the downside by the stakes (Principle 5).

Bait-and-punish borrows the initiative instead: offer the opponent an opening, let them overextend to take it, and punish the overreach on the following turn. You give up the first move to gain a better second one. It trades early tempo for a stronger position later, and it is the answer when a straight alpha strike is too swingy or the opponent is the one who must come to you.

Why this matters

1. Being ahead is a reason to press, not to coast. This is Principle 1's "no coasting" made active. A lead defended passively hands the initiative back: the opponent gets a free turn to set problems while you only react, and objective control is re-contested every round.1 Holding a lead means continuing to force reactions, not sitting on the score.

2. Tempo is the meta-skill that recruits the other four. You concentrate (Principle 4) to remove a unit before it activates; you point threat range (Principle 3) at their scoring to force reactions; you choose the moment to strike or to bait (Principle 5). Tempo is initiative measured across the game, wielding all four together, a way of using the principles, not a fifth one standing beside them.

3. It reframes every later advanced lesson. Target priority, threat overload, the hammer and anvil, the first-turn choice at deployment: each is, underneath, a question about who gets to set the problems. That is why tempo is taught first.

The Art of War Sun Tzu states initiative directly: "The clever combatant imposes his will on the enemy, but does not allow the enemy's will to be imposed on him."2 (Ch. VI, Weak Points and Strong.) That is the definition of holding the initiative. The mechanism for forcing reactions is in the same chapter: "Appear at points which the enemy must hasten to defend; march swiftly to places where you are not expected."2 And on seizing the tempo early rather than arriving on the back foot: "Whoever is first in the field and awaits the coming of the enemy, will be fresh for the fight; whoever is second in the field and has to hasten to battle will arrive exhausted."2 (both Ch. VI.)
Key line "Initiative is who sets the problems; tempo is holding it turn after turn. Remove a unit before it acts and you don't just deny its damage: you delete an activation, a double swing. Force them to spend their turn answering your threats and they never run their own plan. Being ahead is a reason to press tempo, not to coast."
The trap The shallow answer reads tempo as "play fast and aggressive." The deep answer is that tempo is control of who reacts to whom, an economy of activations and forced responses. You can hold the initiative while playing patiently (bait-and-punish), and lose it while attacking hard (swinging at pieces that don't force a response). The tell of a shallow player is "I was aggressive and still lost the game", because aggression that never forces the opponent off their plan isn't tempo, it's just motion.
Heuristic — keep it blunt on purpose "Whose turn is it, really? If they're solving my problems, I hold tempo. If I'm solving theirs, I've lost it, and getting it back is the priority." Blunt enough to fire mid-turn, when a satisfying but ineffective attack is tempting you.

Recall — answer out loud before peeking:

① Why is removing an enemy unit before it activates a "double swing," and not just a kill?

show answer

Because you gain your own unit's output and delete an entire activation from the opponent's turn: that unit now produces zero. You didn't just deny some damage; you subtracted a whole unit's turn. It's the economy of exchanges at its sharpest: a trade they never get to trade back.

② You're 12 VP ahead at the start of your turn. Is pressing threats or playing safe the tempo-correct move, and why?

show answer

Press. Being ahead is Principle 1's "no coasting": a lead is a race, not a bank balance. Sitting on it hands the initiative back: the opponent gets a free turn to set problems while you only react, and objective control is re-contested every round. Holding a lead means continuing to force reactions.

③ In which 40K phase is "kill it before it acts" most literally true, and why that phase specifically?

show answer

The Fight phase, the one place the game genuinely alternates activations (players take turns selecting an eligible unit to fight). Kill their unit before it's selected and it never swings; its activation is gone. In the other phases it's I-go-you-go, so the same effect is coarser: kill on your turn and it does nothing across their whole following turn.

④ You attacked hard every turn but never forced the opponent off the moves that score their mission. Did you hold the initiative?

show answer

No. Initiative is about who reacts to whom, not how aggressive you look. If they were free to run their own plan while you swung, they held the tempo. Your aggression was motion, not initiative. The threat that takes tempo is the one they cannot afford to ignore.
