Advanced Wargaming Theory · Lesson 3

Deception & threat overload

Lesson 2 found the one piece worth removing. This lesson goes offensive with that knowledge: rather than remove their linchpin, shape what the opponent believes and present more decisive threats in a turn than they can cover. This is where Sun Tzu's "all warfare is deception" becomes a set of table moves.

The concept

Three related tools sit here. Baiting and feints — offering a target you want the opponent to take. Sacrificial pieces — chaff whose whole job is to die profitably. And threat overload — presenting more problems in a single turn than the opponent has answers for, so something gets through no matter how they prioritise.

Threat overload is the offensive complement to the capability-mapping skill from the fundamentals. Capability-mapping is defensive: cover the opponent's best move. Overload turns it around — make it impossible for the opponent to cover yours.

Deception controls what they believe; overload controls what they can cover.

Step 1 — Deception: offer a target you want them to take

All warfare is deception, and the tactical form is the bait: a piece placed so the opponent's most attractive move is the one that helps you. They overextend into your counter to take it, or spend a turn killing something you were glad to lose. Feign disorder to draw the commitment, then punish it. A bait exploits the opponent's own justification — they take the move they can most easily justify (the other fundamentals cross-cutting skill, in reverse), and you built that move to be a trap.

Step 2 — Sacrificial pieces: chaff that dies profitably

A sacrificial piece is a cheap unit whose entire job is to die and buy something worth more than itself — screen a charge, block a lane, force the opponent to spend a turn or an activation. This is Principle 2's "a winning trade even though your models die," made proactive: you plan the loss in advance. The chaff wins its trade when the tempo, space, or activation it buys exceeds its points cost.

Step 3 — Threat overload: more problems than answers

Threat overload is presenting several credible threats in one turn so that the opponent, able to answer only some, must let one through. If you threaten three decisive things and they can stop two, one lands regardless of how well they prioritise. The load-bearing word is credible: each threat must be one the opponent cannot afford to ignore (the forcing-reactions idea from Lesson 1). Three threats they can safely ignore is just spreading thin.

Step 4 — Overload is capability-mapping, inverted

The defensive skill maps the opponent's capabilities and covers their best move; being safe against their best is being safe against every worse one. Threat overload runs the same logic from the other side: you present so many answer-forcing threats that no single arrangement of their resources covers them all. Defence covers their best move; offence overloads their coverage. Same map, opposite direction.

Why this matters

1. It extends tempo into information. Lesson 1 was about who reacts to whom; deception and overload are about what they react to and with what beliefs. You are not just forcing reactions — you are forcing the wrong ones, or forcing more than they can make.

2. Overload is not dilution. Multiple threats only overload if each is individually decisive. Spreading force across many weak threats violates concentration and gets answered everywhere. Overload is several concentrated threats, each one a problem they can't ignore.

3. It is the offensive face of the two cross-cutting skills. Baiting weaponises the opponent's justification-distrust failure; overload weaponises capability-mapping against them. The habits that keep you honest, turned outward, become attacks.

The Art of War This is Sun Tzu's most direct statement of deception: "All warfare is based on deception. Hence, when able to attack, we must seem unable; when using our forces, we must seem inactive; when we are near, we must make the enemy believe we are far away; when far away, we must make him believe we are near. Hold out baits to entice the enemy. Feign disorder, and crush him."1 (Ch. I, Laying Plans.) The bait and the feint are named outright.
Key line "Deception shapes what they believe; overload gives them more problems than answers. Bait a move you want them to make, spend chaff that dies profitably, and threaten more decisive things in one turn than they can cover, so one gets through no matter how they prioritise. Capability-mapping covers their best move — overload stops them covering yours."
The trap The shallow answer is "make lots of threats." The deep answer is that each threat must be credible — one they cannot ignore — or it is spreading, not overload, and spreading thin violates concentration. Overload is multiple concentrated threats, not one army diluted across the board. The tell is a player who "threatens everywhere" and gets answered everywhere because no single threat was decisive.
Heuristic — keep it blunt on purpose "More problems than they have turns to solve — and every problem must be one they can't ignore." The second clause is what stops overload collapsing into dilution.

Recall — answer out loud before peeking:

① What separates a real threat-overload from just spreading your army thin?

show answer

Every threat must be credible — decisive enough that they cannot afford to ignore it (forcing reactions, L1). Overload is several concentrated threats they must each answer; spreading is many weak ones they can safely ignore, and it violates concentration (P4).

② How is a sacrificial chaff unit a winning trade if it dies?

show answer

It's Principle 2 made proactive: you plan the loss. The chaff wins its trade when the tempo, space, or activation it buys (screening a charge, blocking a lane, eating a turn) is worth more than its points. Dying is the job, not the failure.

③ How does threat overload relate to the capability-mapping skill?

show answer

It's the inverse. Capability-mapping (defence) covers the opponent's best move. Overload (offence) presents so many answer-forcing threats that no arrangement of their resources covers all of yours. Same map, opposite direction.

④ You threaten three decisive things; the opponent can answer two. What happens, and what condition makes it work?

show answer

One threat lands regardless of how they prioritise. The condition: all three must be credible / can't-ignore. If one was safely ignorable, they answer the two that matter and the overload fails.
