Advanced Wargaming Theory · Lesson 6

List construction as applied theory

Lesson 5 placed the hammer, the anvil, and the reserve on the table. This lesson goes one layer earlier: those pieces have to be bought first. Your army is a set of decisions made before the game, under the same principles — a thesis about how you intend to win.

The concept

A list is a plan, not a pile of strong datasheets. Every point spent is a commitment made before you have seen the board, so a good list is a coherent answer to one question: how does this army score? Reading a list — your own and the opponent's — as a thesis about how to win is where a great deal of competitive improvement actually lives.

The same structures from the in-game lessons become construction constraints. A list must contain a coherent hammer and enough anvil (Lesson 4), cover the full threat spectrum so it can concentrate on any target type (Principle 4), and avoid a single point of failure that folds it when one linchpin dies (Lesson 2, turned on yourself).

List construction is every principle applied one layer earlier — before the game.

Step 1 — A list is a thesis about how to win

The list is pre-game commitment: points spent before a single board is seen. A good list states a plan — "this is how I score, and this is what protects the plan." A collection of individually strong units with no plan connecting them is not a list; it has no thesis to test. The test is blunt: what does this army actually do to move the score-difference?

Step 2 — Does it have a hammer and enough anvil?

The hammer-and-anvil maneuver is a construction requirement, not only an in-game one. A list that is all hammer has nothing to fix the enemy so the blow can land; a list that is all anvil holds the enemy but never wins the decisive exchange. Building the army means checking that both roles are present and that the anvil is enough to let the hammer connect — the same balance Lesson 4 demanded, now decided at the point of purchase.

Step 3 — Threat spectrum and single points of failure

Can the list remove each target type — anti-tank, anti-horde, anti-elite? A list that cannot hurt a whole category hands the opponent a free plan: they lean on the thing you can't answer. This is concentration as a build question — to concentrate on any decisive target, you must be able to hurt every kind. Then internal balance: does the list collapse if one linchpin dies? That is target priority pointed at yourself. A list with a single point of failure loses to a competent opponent's target priority — they take the one piece and the thesis falls apart.

Step 4 — Reading the opponent's list is capability-mapping, early

The capability-mapping skill from the fundamentals runs before deployment even begins. Their list tells you what they can do — where their hammer is, what their anvil is, which target types they cover, and which piece is their linchpin. Reading it as a thesis ("here is how they win") tells you what to deny and what to target, before a single model is placed on the board.

Why this matters

1. The game is partly decided at list-writing. Like deployment, list construction is a phase of the game that happens before the dice — every principle applied one layer earlier. The hammer/anvil balance, the threat spectrum, the single-point-of-failure check, and reading the opponent are all list decisions.

2. Coherence beats raw power. A balanced list with no fatal gap and no single linchpin generally beats a stronger-on-paper list with a hole in its threat spectrum or one death that unravels it.

3. It is the temple calculation. Sun Tzu says the battle is won by the calculations made before it. The list is that calculation written down — and the same reading applied to the opponent's list is how you know what plan you are walking into.

The Art of War Sun Tzu puts the decisive work before the fighting: "Now the general who wins a battle makes many calculations in his temple ere the battle is fought. The general who loses a battle makes but few calculations beforehand. Thus do many calculations lead to victory, and few calculations to defeat: how much more no calculation at all!"1 (Ch. I, Laying Plans.) The list is the temple calculation — the plan settled before a die is rolled.

The trap The shallow answer is "take the strongest units." The deep answer is that a list is a coherent plan, not a pile of good datasheets — balance, a full threat spectrum, and no single point of failure beat raw power. The tell is a player who nets a top list and then can't hurt a target type, or folds the moment their one linchpin dies to a competent opponent's target priority.
Heuristic — keep it blunt on purpose "What is this army's plan to score — and what single death breaks it? Fix that before the game starts."

Recall — answer out loud before peeking:

① Complete the frame: a list is a ______, not a ______.

show answer

A list is a thesis about how to win (a coherent plan to score), not a pile of individually strong units. If nothing connects the units into a plan, there's no thesis to test.

② Name two list requirements that come straight from earlier lessons.

show answer

Any two of: a coherent hammer and enough anvil (L4); the full threat spectrum — can hurt anti-tank/horde/elite (P4); no single point of failure that folds the list when one linchpin dies (L2 turned on yourself).

③ Reading the opponent's list is which skill, run when?

show answer

Capability-mapping (the fundamentals cross-cutting skill), run before deployment begins. Their list tells you what they can do — hammer, anvil, threat spectrum, linchpin — so you know what to deny and target before placing a model.

④ A strong-on-paper list can't hurt vehicles. What does that hand the opponent?

show answer

A free plan: they lean on the target type you can't answer (armour) and score off it unimpeded. A gap in the threat spectrum is a hole a good opponent builds their whole game around.
