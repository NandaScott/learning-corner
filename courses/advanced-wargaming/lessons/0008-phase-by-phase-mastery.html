Advanced Wargaming Theory · Lesson 8

Phase-by-phase mastery: the craft layer

Every principle in this course meets the table here, in millimetres. The movement phase is the most important and least flashy phase: most of threat range is executed in it, with no dice, and the exact spot each model ends up decides what falls in range and what is exposed. This is frequently what separates players who understand the game from players who win it.

The concept

The craft layer is where theory becomes geometry. Four things live here: the primacy of the movement phase; unit coherency and model spacing; the geometry of charges, pile-ins, and consolidations; and model-by-model placement that controls precisely what is in range. The rules are exact numbers, and the game is won inside their tolerances.

This is Principle 3 at the resolution of a single base, threat range executed one model at a time, and it is where the screening bubbles of Lesson 5 are actually drawn.

The dice phases mostly confirm what the movement phase already decided.

Step 1 — The movement phase is the most important, and the least flashy

Most of threat range (Principle 3) is executed in the movement phase, and it has no dice at all: it is pure positioning. The player who wins the movement phase decides which fights happen and on what terms; the shooting and fighting mostly confirm what the movement already set.

Step 2 — Coherency and spacing

A unit is in coherency while every model is within 2" horizontally of at least one other model in the unit, and within 9" horizontally of every other model.1 That 2" of slack is a lever. Spread the models to blunt blast weapons and to stretch the unit's footprint, more board covered, harder to fully surround or to trap out of coherency. Bunch them to hold coherency after losses, or to fit the whole unit behind a line-of-sight blocker. How you use the slack decides what is exposed.

Step 3 — Charge, pile-in, and consolidation geometry

A charge is rolled on 2D6, and the charging unit must end within engagement range, 2" horizontally, of every unit it declared against.1 Pile-in (before models fight) and consolidate (after) each move a model up to 3".1 This geometry is where a combat's real result is set: the pile-in that drags more models into range, the charge angled to tag a second unit, the consolidate that ends the fight sitting on an objective or pulled back out of a counter-charge. The dice roll the wounds; the geometry decides who is even in the fight and what you control when it ends.

Step 4 — Model-by-model placement

Within a unit, which model sits where controls exactly what falls in range and what is exposed. Pull the character back behind the body so it can't be reached; put the durable model at the front to eat the first hits; place the closest model so the unit's threat range just reaches the target while the rest stay behind a blocker. Screening and deep-strike denial are drawn the same way: a screen where every point of the zone is within 9" of a screening model leaves the enemy no legal place to arrive.1 That denial bubble is measured in inches, model by model.

Why this matters

1. It is every principle at table resolution. Threat range is executed model by model in the movement phase; concentration needs coherency and charge geometry to actually mass at the point of contact; the screening bubbles from deployment are drawn here in inches; and where models end up now sets who can act on whom next turn, which is tempo.

2. It is the layer that separates understanding from winning. The theory of this whole course is regenerable once you hold the principles, but the game is won in the movement phase, in the millimetres of coherency, charge angles, and denial bubbles. Two players who understand the theory equally are separated by who sweats the geometry.

3. It closes the course. Tempo, target priority, deception, the hammer and anvil, deployment, the list, and variance all terminate here, on the table, in the exact positions of models. This is where all of it is finally spent.

The Art of War Sun Tzu's chapter on maneuvering is the craft layer: "The difficulty of tactical maneuvering consists in turning the devious into the direct, and misfortune into gain."2 And on the movement itself: "Let your rapidity be that of the wind, your compactness that of the forest."2 (both Ch. VII, Maneuvering.) Maneuvering, the movement phase, is where advantage is manufactured before a blow is struck.

The trap The shallow answer treats movement as "just getting into position for the real phases." The deep answer is that movement is the decisive phase: the dice phases mostly confirm what positioning already set, and games are won in the millimetres of coherency, charge geometry, and denial bubbles. The tell is a player who rushes the movement phase to get to shooting, then wonders why every fight was on bad terms.
Heuristic — keep it blunt on purpose "Win the movement phase and the dice mostly confirm it. Every model's exact spot decides what's in range. Sweat the millimetres."

Recall — answer out loud before peeking:

① Why is the movement phase the most important phase?

show answer

Most of threat range (P3) is executed there, with no dice: it decides which fights happen and on what terms. The shooting and fighting mostly confirm what movement already set. It's the least flashy phase and the decisive one.

② State the 40K coherency rule, and when you'd spread versus bunch.

show answer

Every model within 2" of at least one other model and 9" of every other model. Spread to blunt blast weapons and stretch the footprint (cover more board, harder to surround); bunch to hold coherency after losses or to fit behind a line-of-sight blocker.

③ Give the charge, pile-in, and consolidate distances, and say what consolidate is for.

show answer

Charge = 2D6, ending within engagement range (2"). Pile-in and consolidate = up to 3" each. Consolidate (after fighting) is used to end the combat sitting on an objective, tag another unit, or pull back out of a counter-charge.

④ How does a deep-strike-denial screen actually work, in geometry?

show answer

Arriving units must be set up more than 9" from your models. If every point of a zone is within 9" of a screening model, there's no legal place for the enemy to arrive, so the denial bubble is drawn model by model, in inches.
