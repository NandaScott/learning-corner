Advanced Wargaming Theory · Lesson 7

Risk & probability as a felt skill

The list is set and the plan is chosen. Whether the plan holds now depends on the dice, and the skill is not computing odds but feeling variance well enough to build plans that survive bad luck. The core discipline: make decisions robust to bad luck rather than reliant on good luck.

The concept

This goes past a rough estimation model into internalising variance, expected value, and robustness as a felt sense. The practical heart is small and uncomfortable: an "average" outcome fails roughly half the time. A plan built on the average is a plan that works about half the time on the thing you needed most.

Everything else follows from planning on the range instead of the average. Commit with enough margin that variance cannot rob a needed result (Principle 4); weight the worst case when the stakes are high (Principle 5); and spend your guaranteed-effect resources where variance is most dangerous.

Risk is Principles 4 and 5 fused into a feel for the distribution.

Step 1 — The average fails about half the time

"On average I kill it" means you kill it roughly half the time: the average is the centre of a distribution, not a floor. Expected value tells you where the middle sits; it says nothing about how often you land short of it. Internalising that gap is the felt skill: the difference between a player who plans on the expected result and one who plans on the whole spread of results.

Step 2 — Robust to bad luck, not reliant on good luck

A robust plan wins across the likely range of rolls; a fragile plan wins only if the dice cooperate. The discipline is to prefer the line that still works when the rolls come in below average. A plan that depends on above-average rolls loses whenever they fail to arrive. This is also why guaranteed-effect tools (auto-hits, mortal wounds, re-rolls) are prized: they convert "probably" into "certainly" exactly where certainty is worth paying for.

Step 3 — Margin: commit enough that variance can't rob the result

When you actually need a result, commit enough force that a below-average roll still clears the bar. This is the floor of the target band from Principle 4, "enough to remove it with margin", and the margin scales with the stakes. Committing exactly the average is planning to fail half the time on the very thing you needed to happen. More dice than the mean requires is buying robustness, not waste, when the result is one you need.

Step 4 — Spend certainty where variance bites hardest

Guaranteed-effect resources are most valuable on low-volume, high-stakes rolls: the one charge you must make, the single save that decides the game, because low-volume rolls carry the highest variance and the law of averages does not protect them. Spending certainty on high-volume rolls (a forty-shot volley) is waste: across many dice the average already holds, so the outcome is close to expectation without help. Certainty belongs on the pivotal single roll, not the mass roll.

Why this matters

1. It is Principles 4 and 5 turned into a feel. Margin (P4) makes a needed result robust; worst-case weighting and variance-appetite (P5) tune how much margin and how much swing. And it explains why guaranteed tools are so valuable: they buy certainty precisely where variance is most dangerous.

2. Variance appetite flips with the scoreboard. Ahead, take the low-floor line that protects the result even at the cost of upside; behind, take the swingy high-ceiling play, because the safe line just loses slowly. This is the P5 dial, and it means "the correct amount of risk" is never fixed: it reads off the score.

3. It keeps "unlucky" honest. Most losses blamed on dice were fragile plans built on the average. Planning on the range converts a lot of "bad luck" back into "bad commitment."

The Art of War Sun Tzu states robustness directly: "The art of war teaches us to rely not on the likelihood of the enemy's not coming, but on our own readiness to receive him; not on the chance of his not attacking, but rather on the fact that we have made our position unassailable."1 (Ch. VIII, Variation in Tactics.) Do not rely on the good roll or the enemy's mistake; build the position so it holds regardless.
Key line "Make decisions robust to bad luck, not reliant on good. 'On average I kill it' means about half the time: plan on the range, not the average. Commit with margin so variance can't rob a needed result, and spend guaranteed-effect tools on the low-volume, high-stakes rolls where variance bites hardest, not the high-volume rolls the average already protects."
The trap The shallow answer is "I had the odds, I got unlucky." The deep answer is that if you needed a coin-flip to go your way, you built a fragile plan: robustness means winning on a below-average roll, and certainty belongs on the one pivotal roll, not the forty that don't matter. The tell is a player who commits exactly-average force to a must-kill target, whiffs, and calls it variance.
Heuristic — keep it blunt on purpose "Does this plan still work on a below-average roll? If not, add margin or don't need it. Certainty goes on the one roll that decides, not the forty that don't."

Recall — answer out loud before peeking:

① What does "on average I kill it" actually tell you about how often it happens?

show answer

Roughly half the time: the average is the centre of a distribution, not a floor. It says where the middle sits, not how often you fall short. Planning on it is planning to fail about half the time on the needed result.

② "Robust" versus "reliant": one line each.

show answer

Robust: the plan wins across the likely range of rolls, including below average. Reliant: the plan wins only if the dice cooperate. Robust-to-bad-luck is the winning posture; reliant-on-good-luck loses on the table.

③ Where do guaranteed-effect tools (re-rolls, auto-hits, CP) give the most value, and why?

show answer

On low-volume, high-stakes rolls: the one charge or save that decides the game, because low-volume rolls have the highest variance and the law of averages doesn't protect them. On high-volume rolls the average already holds, so certainty there is wasted.

④ You have one re-roll. A must-make 8" charge decides the game; you also have a forty-shot volley. Where does the re-roll go?

show answer

The charge. It's the low-volume, high-stakes roll where variance is most dangerous and the outcome is pivotal. The forty-shot volley is protected by its own volume: its result will land near expectation without the re-roll.

Primary source — read this one Read Sun Tzu's Chapter VIII, Variation in Tactics, in the Giles translation. The "rely on our own readiness, not on the enemy's not coming" passage is robustness stated plainly. For the probability side, any clear treatment of expected value versus variance (and why small samples swing) will sharpen the felt skill; the point is to internalise the spread, not memorise tables.
