Advanced Wargaming Theory · Lesson 4

The hammer and anvil

The oldest maneuver in land warfare, and the frame beneath much of 40K. The anvil fixes the enemy so they cannot move freely; the hammer then lands the decisive blow from a direction they cannot defend. The single lesson to take: a hammer needs an anvil.

The concept

The maneuver has two roles. The anvil fixes the enemy: it holds them, pins them, or gives them a problem they cannot ignore or easily leave. The hammer is the mobile, high-output striking force that then delivers the winning blow. "Hammer" is a role, not a datasheet: it is your decisive offensive package, whatever unit fills it.

This is concentration of force made into a physical object. Holding the hammer hidden until the moment is the deferral of Principle 5; choosing what the hammer hits is target priority; fixing the enemy is forcing a reaction. The frame ties the earlier lessons into one shape.

The hammer is the exciting half; the anvil is the load-bearing half.

Step 1 — Two roles: the anvil fixes, the hammer strikes

The anvil's job is to remove the enemy's freedom to reposition: it holds them, or presents something they cannot walk away from. The hammer's job is to arrive from a direction they cannot defend and cross the removal cliff on a decisive target. Sun Tzu names these the direct and indirect force: the direct engages and holds, the indirect delivers the win.

Step 2 — Hammer is a role, not a datasheet

Any unit that fills four marks is a hammer: high removal output (it crosses the removal cliff, Principle 4), mobility (it chooses the point of the strike, threat range plus target priority), durability (it arrives intact to swing), and high cost (which is exactly why losing it prematurely is catastrophic, and why Principle 5 says hide it until the moment). The role is defined by function; the datasheet is just whatever currently performs it.

Step 3 — A hammer needs an anvil

This is the load-bearing claim. Unpinned, a mobile enemy simply avoids your scary unit: they dance away while the rest of their army scores, and your most expensive piece chases shadows all game. The anvil is what removes their option to avoid: it fixes them (or fixes their attention on a problem they must solve) so the hammer can land where they cannot defend. A hammer without an anvil is a threat that is trivial to dodge.

Step 4 — Against a shooting army, reinterpret the anvil

You cannot pin a gunline in melee if it kites away. So against a shooting army the anvil changes tools while keeping its job. It becomes objective pressure: make it cost them the game to stay back, so they must come forward to contest or lose on points (Principle 1); and space denial: take away the room they need to kite (Principle 3). The anvil still removes the enemy's freedom to avoid; the tool shifts from a melee pin to a positional one.

Why this matters

1. It is the structure that combines the principles into a shape. Concentration builds the hammer; space control builds the anvil; timing decides the swing; target priority picks what it hits. The hammer and anvil is the earlier principles assembled into the oldest maneuver there is.

2. It reframes the failing deathstar. "My expensive unit isn't performing" almost always means it has no anvil, the enemy is free to avoid it. The fix is rarely a bigger hammer; it is an anvil that takes away the dodge.

3. It is a list-building constraint. If the hammer and anvil is how you win in the game, then the list has to contain both, which is where the next-but-one lesson goes.

The Art of War Sun Tzu's direct-and-indirect force is the hammer and anvil exactly: "In battle, there are not more than two methods of attack — the direct and the indirect; yet these two in combination give rise to an endless series of maneuvers."1 And on which does what: "In all fighting, the direct method may be used for joining battle, but indirect methods will be needed in order to secure victory."1 (both Ch. V, Energy.) The direct force (zheng) joins and holds, the anvil; the indirect force (qi) secures the victory, the hammer.
Key line "The anvil fixes; the hammer strikes. Hammer is a role, not a datasheet: output, mobility, durability, cost. A hammer needs an anvil, or a mobile enemy just avoids it while their army scores. Against a gunline the anvil becomes objective pressure and space denial: make staying back cost the game, and take away the room to kite."
The trap The shallow answer is "the hammer is my melee deathstar." The deep answer is that the hammer is a functional role, and the load-bearing half of the maneuver is the anvil: an unanviled hammer chases shadows all game. And against a shooting army the anvil is not a combat unit at all; it is objective pressure and space denial. The tell is a player who buys a huge hammer, brings nothing to fix the enemy, and never connects.
Heuristic — keep it blunt on purpose "What's fixing them while my hammer moves? No anvil, no hit." Ask it every time the expensive unit is about to commit.

Recall — answer out loud before peeking:

① State the anvil's job and the hammer's job, one line each.

show answer

Anvil: fix the enemy, remove their freedom to reposition (hold/pin, or a problem they can't leave). Hammer: the mobile, high-output striker that lands the decisive blow from a direction they can't defend.

② What four marks make a unit a "hammer," and why is it a role rather than a datasheet?

show answer

High removal output, mobility (chooses the strike), durability (arrives intact), high cost (losing it early is catastrophic). It's a role because any datasheet meeting those functions fills it: the maneuver is defined by function, not by a named unit.

③ Why does a hammer without an anvil usually fail?

show answer

Unpinned, a mobile enemy just avoids it, dances away while the rest of their army scores, and your most expensive piece chases shadows. Nothing removes their option to dodge, so the blow never lands where it matters.

④ Against a shooting army that kites, how does the anvil change, and what stays the same?

show answer

The job stays: remove their freedom to avoid. The tool changes from a melee pin to objective pressure (make staying back lose them the game, P1) plus space denial (take the room to kite, P3). A positional pin instead of a physical one.
