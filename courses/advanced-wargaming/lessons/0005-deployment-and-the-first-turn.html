Advanced Wargaming Theory · Lesson 5

Deployment & the first-turn metagame

A large fraction of the game is decided before a die is rolled. Deployment is where the earlier principles cash out physically: it is where "win first, then go to battle" happens on the table. The hammer and anvil from Lesson 4 is placed here, hidden and forward.

The concept

Deployment is a sequence of commitments made with the most information you will ever have about the board and the least about the dice. It runs on four decisions: read the mission and terrain before placing anything; decide whether to prioritise the first turn or give it away; place units as threat-range management; and use screening, zoning, and reserves to shape what the opponent can even attempt on turn one.

In 40K the mission itself sets which player takes the first turn, and the detailed deployment procedure is part of the mission's rules rather than the core rulebook.1 So this lesson teaches the decisions, not a fixed roll-off: the reasoning holds wherever the choice appears.

Deployment is the game's first tempo decision and its first act of concentration.

Step 1 — Read the mission and terrain before placing anything

Before a model goes down, read the two things that don't move: the mission (what scores, and where the objectives sit) and the terrain (what blocks line of sight, what channels movement). Deployment is a response to those. Placing units before reading the board is committing blind: you cannot manage threat ranges you haven't mapped, or contest objectives you haven't located.

Step 2 — The first turn: take it or give it

Whether going first helps you is an army question, not a universal good. It is the Lesson 1 choice made at setup: prioritise the first turn to alpha strike: set the first problem and seize tempo. Or give it away and play reactively, letting the opponent commit first so you can punish the overreach. Going first is worth a great deal to an alpha army and little, sometimes less than nothing, to a reactive one. Where you have any influence over the first turn, spend it according to which your army wants.

Step 3 — Deployment as threat-range management

Every unit you place declares a threat range (Principle 3). Deployment is choosing what you expose, units inside the opponent's first-turn threat, versus what you hold back behind blockers or out of range. Expose the anvil and the chaff; hold the hammer. This is Lesson 4's "hide the hammer" done physically: the expensive striker should not sit where the opponent can hit it before it swings, which is also Principle 5's rule about not committing the irreversible piece early.

Step 4 — Screening, zoning, and reserves

Three tools shape what the opponent can do on turn one. Screening: cheap units placed to deny space, so the enemy cannot deep-strike or charge where you've covered (arriving units must be set up more than 9" from your models,1 and cannot come through a screen). Zoning: shaping where the enemy is willing to go by what you threaten. Reserves: units held off the table are pre-committed optionality (Principle 5): they cannot be shot on turn one, and they let you place a threat after seeing the opponent's move, deferring the commitment. Reserves physically strip targets out of the opponent's first turn.

Why this matters

1. It is "win first" made physical. Principle 1 and Sun Tzu both say the position that decides the game is secured before the fighting. Deployment is where that positioning literally happens: threat ranges, the hammer/anvil placement, the first-turn tempo choice, and reserves as deferral all cash out at setup.

2. Going first is not free value. The reflex "always take the first turn" loses games for reactive armies. The first-turn decision is a real choice keyed to what your list wants to do.

3. Deployment answers to the list. You can only deploy a hammer and anvil, or hold a meaningful reserve, if the list was built to contain them, which is the next lesson.

The Art of War Deployment is where Sun Tzu's "win first" becomes physical: "the victorious strategist only seeks battle after the victory has been won, whereas he who is destined to defeat first fights and afterwards looks for victory."2 (Ch. IV, Tactical Dispositions.) And on the advantage of setting up first and on your terms: "Whoever is first in the field and awaits the coming of the enemy, will be fresh for the fight; whoever is second in the field and has to hasten to battle will arrive exhausted."2 (Ch. VI, Weak Points and Strong.)
Key line "Half the game is decided before a die is rolled. Read the mission and terrain, then deploy as threat-range management: expose the anvil and chaff, hold the hammer. The first-turn choice is a tempo decision: take it to strike first, give it to punish. Reserves are pre-committed optionality that strip targets out of the enemy's first turn."
The trap The shallow answer is "deploy your army, then start playing." The deep answer is that deployment is where you win first: you read the board before placing, and going first is not universally good: a reactive army wants to give it away. The tell is a player who deploys the hammer forward where it dies on turn one, or who reflexively takes the first turn regardless of what their list wants to do.
Heuristic — keep it blunt on purpose "Read the board, then place. Hide the hammer, expose the anvil. First turn: do I want to set the problem, or punish theirs?"

Recall — answer out loud before peeking:

① Why is taking the first turn not always the right choice?

show answer

It's army-dependent (the L1 alpha-vs-reactive choice). Going first is worth a lot to an alpha-strike army and little, sometimes negative, to a reactive one, which would rather give it away, let the opponent overextend, and punish. First turn is a tempo decision, not free value.

② What do you expose versus hold back at deployment, and why?

show answer

Expose the anvil and chaff; hold the hammer (out of range / behind blockers). The expensive striker shouldn't sit where it can be hit before it swings: that's hiding the hammer (L4) and not committing the irreversible piece early (P5), done physically as threat-range management.

③ What does holding a unit in reserve buy you?

show answer

Pre-committed optionality (P5): it can't be shot on turn one, and you commit it after seeing the opponent's move, deferring the decision to when you have more information. Reserves physically strip targets out of the enemy's first turn.

④ Deployment is which foundational principle made physical?

show answer

Principle 1's "win first, then fight": the positioning that decides the game is secured before combat. Deployment is where threat ranges, the hammer/anvil placement, and the first-turn choice literally happen on the table.
