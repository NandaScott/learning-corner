Advanced Wargaming Theory · Lesson 2

Target priority

Lesson 1 established that removing a unit takes tempo — you delete an activation. This lesson answers the question that leaves open: which unit? Target priority is the discipline of identifying the one enemy piece whose removal most collapses their plan.

The concept

The highest-value target is rarely the scariest gun. Value comes from Principle 1: an action is worth exactly how much it moves the score-difference. Applied to removal, that means the decisive target is the one whose death does the most damage to their plan to score — often a buff character, a force multiplier, or a linchpin, not the unit with the biggest guns.

The distinction this lesson turns on: dangerous is what a unit can do to you; decisive is what its removal does to them. The most violent target and the most valuable target are usually not the same piece.

Steps 1–3 pick the target; step 4 is how 40K makes reaching it part of the problem.

Step 1 — Dangerous is not decisive

A unit that outputs enormous damage but was going to whiff, die anyway, or never touch the scoring is a low-value target — removing it barely moves the score-difference. A cheap character quietly buffing three units is a high-value target, because its removal degrades all three. Danger measures the threat to you; value measures the leverage its death has on their plan. Confusing the two is the most common target-priority error.

Step 2 — Read what a unit outputs, then weight it by role

Threat assessment is step one: what does this unit do, to what, this turn? But raw output is only half the question. The full question multiplies output by centrality: if this were gone, how much of their plan degrades? A high-output unit that acts alone costs them only its own output. A modest unit that enables others costs them a slice of everything it touches.

Step 3 — Find the linchpin

The linchpin is the force multiplier — the buff character, the aura, the re-roll or leader ability that makes several other units work. Removing it does not just subtract its own output; it subtracts a fraction of every unit it was enabling. This is the removal cliff from Principle 4 aimed at leverage: the buff's death cliffs the whole cluster at once. That leverage is why the linchpin beats the big gun — same removal effort, far more of their plan collapses.

Step 4 — The 40K wrinkle: linchpins hide, so create the shot

In 40K the linchpin is usually protected. A Character with the Leader ability attaches to a Bodyguard unit before the battle, and while it is attached the enemy cannot pick it out — attacks target the combined unit, so the Character is shielded by the models around it.1 So removing the linchpin means one of: grinding through the bodyguard unit to force its death, or catching the Character exposed (a Lone Operative out of screen, or a unit whittled down to the character). Target priority in 40K is therefore partly about creating the shot at the linchpin, not simply declaring it the target.

Why this matters

1. It is the bridge from Principle 1 to Principle 4. Principle 1 says value is what moves the score-difference; target priority applies that to removal — the decisive target is the one whose removal moves it most. Principle 4 then says put your whole effort on that one thing. Target priority is the step that tells concentration what to concentrate on.

2. The tempting target and the correct target diverge. The board keeps offering the menacing tank while a cheap character runs the game from behind a screen. The discipline is resisting the violent target for the quiet linchpin — and, sometimes, recognising that the scary gun genuinely is the linchpin. You evaluate by plan-collapse, not by menace.

3. It sets up threat overload. Once you can name the decisive targets, the next question is offensive: instead of removing their one linchpin, can you threaten several decisive things at once so they cannot cover them all? That is the next lesson.

The Art of War Sun Tzu ranks attacking the enemy's plan above attacking their army: "the highest form of generalship is to balk the enemy's plans; the next best is to prevent the junction of the enemy's forces; the next in order is to attack the enemy's army in the field; and the worst policy of all is to besiege walled cities."2 (Ch. III, Attack by Stratagem.) Removing the linchpin balks the plan itself rather than merely trading blows with the army — the higher form.
Key line "Target priority is picking the one piece whose removal most collapses their plan — usually the force multiplier, not the scariest gun. Dangerous is what a unit does to you; decisive is what its removal does to them. Concentrate on the decisive one, take it before it acts — and in 40K, expect to create the shot, because the linchpin hides inside a unit."
The trap The shallow answer shoots the biggest threat. The deep answer is that the biggest threat to you is usually not the highest-value target — value is leverage on their plan, not menace. The tell is a player who dumps the army into the scariest tank while a cheap buff-character keeps the rest of the enemy running. Don't overcorrect: sometimes the big gun is the linchpin. The rule is to evaluate every candidate by how much of their plan dies with it.
Heuristic — keep it blunt on purpose "If this died, how much of their plan dies with it? Kill the highest answer, not the highest damage." Blunt enough to fire when a menacing but low-leverage target is begging to be shot.

Recall — answer out loud before peeking:

① Define "dangerous" and "decisive" as they apply to a target, in one line each.

show answer

Dangerous = what the unit can do to you (its threat/output). Decisive = what its removal does to them (how much of their plan collapses). The most violent target and the most valuable one are usually different pieces.

② Why does removing a cheap buff-character usually beat removing the biggest gun?

show answer

Leverage. The linchpin's death doesn't just subtract its own output — it subtracts a fraction of every unit it enabled. That's the removal cliff (P4) aimed at a whole cluster: same removal effort, far more of their plan collapses.

③ In 40K, why is the buff-character often hard to remove, and what does that add to target priority?

show answer

A Leader Character attached to a Bodyguard unit can't be picked out — attacks hit the combined unit. So target priority isn't just naming the target; it's creating the shot — grinding through the bodyguard, or catching the character exposed (Lone Operative, or unit whittled down).

④ You can remove either a menacing tank that acts alone, or a cheap character buffing three units. Which, and why?

show answer

The character, unless the tank is itself decisive to their plan. Value is plan-collapse: the tank costs them one unit's output; the character costs them a slice of three. Kill the highest plan-collapse, not the highest menace.

Push on it. Ask: "how do I tell a linchpin from a unit that just looks important?" or "when is the scary gun actually the right target?" — both sharpen the dangerous/decisive line.
