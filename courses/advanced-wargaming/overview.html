Advanced Wargaming Theory

The sequel to Wargaming Fundamentals. The five principles told you what a move is worth and how to move the score-difference efficiently. This course teaches the second tier built on them — tempo, target priority, deception, the hammer and anvil, deployment, the list, the dice, and the millimetres — each one a specific firing of the five, framed with Warhammer 40,000 (11th edition) and Sun Tzu's Art of War. Assumes the five principles cold.

Part 1 · Controlling the game

The meta-skill that deepens all five foundational principles at once: who is setting the problems.

L1 Tempo and initiativeInitiative is who sets the problem; tempo is holding it over time. An activation is a resource — remove a unit before it acts and you delete one (a double swing). Forcing reactions, and the alpha-strike vs bait-and-punish choice. →

Part 2 · Choosing and overloading targets

Given that removal takes tempo — which piece, and how do you give the opponent more problems than they can answer? These two pair.

L2 Target priorityThe one piece whose removal most collapses their plan — the linchpin, not the scariest gun. Dangerous versus decisive, and why 40K linchpins hide inside units so you must create the shot. → L3 Deception & threat overloadBaiting and feints; sacrificial chaff that dies profitably; presenting more credible threats in one turn than the opponent has answers for. Overload is capability-mapping, inverted. →

Part 3 · Structure and setup

The maneuver frame beneath much of 40K, and the phase where half the game is decided before a die is rolled.

L4 The hammer and anvilThe anvil fixes the enemy; the mobile hammer lands the blow. A hammer needs an anvil, or it chases shadows. Against a gunline the anvil becomes objective pressure and space denial. Sun Tzu's direct (zheng) and indirect (qi) force. → L5 Deployment & the first-turn metagameRead mission and terrain before placing anything; take or give the first turn; deployment as threat-range management; screening, zoning, and reserves as pre-committed optionality. "Win first, then go to battle." →

Part 4 · The list, the dice, the millimetres

The army as a set of pre-game decisions, the felt skill of variance, and where theory meets the table.

L6 List construction as applied theoryYour army is a thesis about how to win. A coherent hammer and enough anvil; the full threat spectrum; internal balance versus folding when one linchpin dies. Reading their list is capability-mapping, early. → L7 Risk & probability as a felt skillMake decisions robust to bad luck, not reliant on good. "On average" fails about half the time; commit with margin; spend guaranteed-effect tools on the low-volume, high-stakes rolls where variance bites. → L8 Phase-by-phase mastery: the craft layerThe movement phase as the most important and least flashy; coherency (2"/9") and spacing; charge (2D6), pile-in and consolidate (3") geometry; placing models to control exactly what falls in range. →
