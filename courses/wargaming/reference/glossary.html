Glossary

The canonical vocabulary of the course. Every lesson uses these words in exactly these senses — if a term feels slippery in a lesson, its precise meaning lives here. Grouped by the principle that owns it.

The axiom — value (Principle 1)

TermMeaning
Win conditionThe single defined metric a wargame optimises. In 40K it is the mission score (Victory Points), not models destroyed. All value derives from it.
Mission score / Victory Points (VP)The 40K win condition made numeric. Scored from primary and secondary missions — mostly by controlling terrain objectives, with control recomputed continuously (end of each phase and turn). In 11th edition the primary mission is set by the players' force dispositions.
Objective Control (OC)The model characteristic that decides who controls a terrain objective: sum the OC of each side's models in range; higher total controls it. This — not kills — is the engine of the mission score.
Body countModels or points destroyed. Has no intrinsic value — it matters only insofar as it protects your scoring or denies theirs. "I killed more and lost" = optimising body count instead of the win condition.
Score-differenceYour VP total minus the opponent's. The only quantity that decides the game. Everything is measured as its movement of this number.
Deny = scoreBecause only the difference decides, taking a point off the opponent is worth exactly as much as scoring one. Same currency; choose whichever is cheaper right now.
No coastingThe mission scores every round and the opponent is always proactive, so a lead is a snapshot of a race that never pauses — not a stored balance. Holding a lead is active work.

Cost — the economy of exchanges (Principle 2)

TermMeaning
Exchange rateWhat an action costs versus what it returns, both measured in movement of the score-difference. The only question that matters about any "purchase."
Winning tradeA spend that moves the score-difference in your favour — even if your models die. Cheap models that die but buy a turn of position can win their trade.
Opportunity costThe best thing the same resources could have done instead. The true cost of any action. A no-losses kill can still be a bad trade if that unit was needed elsewhere.
OverpayingSpending a premium resource on a cheap problem — a losing trade dressed up as a victory. Caps the top of how much force a task deserves.

Space — threat range & control (Principle 3)

TermMeaning
Threat rangeA unit's movement plus its reach (gun range or charge distance): the whole area it can affect this turn. The atom of board space.
Control (of space)A unit controls its entire threat range whether or not it ever fires, because a rational opponent must respect that footprint. The board is a map of overlapping threat ranges.
Contested spaceThe overlap where both players' threat ranges cover the same ground — where fights happen and games are decided. Space only you threaten is yours; only they threaten is theirs.
Line of sight / blockerWhat breaks the symmetry of threat: a unit behind a blocker sits inside the enemy's threat range but is not actually threatened, because they cannot draw a line to it. Lets you threaten without being threatened.
One-turn-forward readingBecause play is turn-based, safety is judged against where the enemy can be after they move (project each unit its full movement), not where they sit now. A spot safe from current positions but open after they advance is a trap.

Mass — concentration of force (Principle 4)

TermMeaning
Concentration of forceBringing more strength to bear at the decisive point than the enemy can answer there — local superiority, not overall superiority.
Nonlinear output / removal cliffA unit does ~100% of its job right up until it dies, then zero. Removal crosses that cliff; chipping does not. This is why concentration works.
Local superiorityBeing many-against-few at the point of contact, achieved by massing your whole against a fraction of theirs — even without more models overall.
Going thinThe price of concentration: you cannot be strong everywhere, so massing at the decisive point requires being deliberately weak elsewhere. The mechanism, not a flaw.
Target bandThe window of correct commitment: enough to remove the target with margin (bottom edge — is anything actually dying?), and not a scrap more (top edge — the sledgehammer question). Margin scales with the stakes.

Timing — commitment under uncertainty (Principle 5)

TermMeaning
Commitment under uncertaintyThe capstone: when to act on a conclusion. A correct conclusion executed at the wrong moment becomes a blunder.
DeferralDelaying a decision so it is made with more information. Sequence your turn so information-revealing actions come first, irreversible commitments last.
Superposition of threatsAn uncommitted unit threatens several places at once and drains the opponent's certainty — until the instant it collapses into one fixed result. Deferral's payoff.
TempoWhat deferral costs. Windows close, so the skill is landing on the last moment a commitment is still fully effective — not earlier, not later.
ReversibilityHow takeable-back a move is. Cheap reversible moves can go early on partial information; irreversible ones you defer until forced or nearly certain.
Variance appetiteTuning risk to the scoreboard: behind, take swingy high-ceiling plays (the safe line just loses slowly); ahead, take low-floor plays that protect the lead even at the cost of upside.

Cross-cutting skills

TermMeaning
Model by capabilityMap what the opponent can do (their visible threat ranges), not what you predict they will do. Assume their best available move toward the mission; being safe against their best play is being safe against every worse one.
Justification distrustReasoning is a lawyer — it builds a persuasive case for whatever you already wanted (usually the exciting kill). The blunt heuristics work because they are too simple to argue with. The smoother the justification, the harder you check it.
The chainThe load-bearing order in which the five principles fire before any move: value → cost → space → concentration → timing. Lost mid-turn? Drop to the top and walk down.
