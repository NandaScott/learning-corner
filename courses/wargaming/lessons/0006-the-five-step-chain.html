Wargaming Fundamentals · Lesson 6

The five-step chain

You have the five principles. This lesson assembles them into one thing: an ordered chain you run before committing to a move. The order is not a summary convenience. It is load-bearing, because each step needs the answer from the step above it, and every specific piece of tactical advice you've ever heard is just one of these five firing in a particular board state.

The concept

The five principles are not a list you pick from; they are a sequence you run top to bottom. Value decides what is worth doing at all. Cost prices it. Space says where it happens and whether it's safe. Concentration sizes it. Timing fires it. Run in that order, they turn a board full of options into a single defensible move, and run out of order they don't work, because you cannot price a move before you know it's worth doing, or time a decision you haven't made yet.

The chain

Run this before touching a model. Each question depends on the answer to the one above it:

  1. VALUE          What moves the scoreboard this turn — mine up or theirs down?
                       │  (gates everything: no value, no move)
                       ▼
  2. COST           Could I get the same score-effect cheaper?
                       │  (exchange rate, opportunity cost)
                       ▼
  3. SPACE          What do I control vs expose — read one turn forward,
                       │  against their best available option?
                       ▼
  4. CONCENTRATION  Am I bringing enough to remove it with margin, and not a scrap more?
                       │  (the target band: floor and ceiling)
                       ▼
  5. TIMING         Do I have to commit now, or can I learn more first?
                          (fire on the last effective moment)

If you feel lost mid-turn, drop to the top and walk down. The first question you can't answer cleanly is the one that's actually stuck.

Step 1 — Value (Lesson 1)

What moves the score-difference this turn, mine up or theirs down? This gates everything below it. If a move doesn't increase your score or suppress theirs, the chain stops here and you find a different move. Nothing downstream matters until this answers yes, because cost, space, and force are all machinery for moving a number you haven't yet confirmed is worth moving.

Step 2 — Cost (Lesson 2)

Given the move is worth doing, could you get the same score-effect for less? Price it as an exchange rate and include opportunity cost. This step depends on the first: you can only ask "cheaper for the same effect" once you know what effect you're buying. It's where the tempting move that scores the same as a cheaper one gets demoted.

Step 3 — Space (Lesson 3)

Where does this happen, what do you control versus expose, and is the position safe read one turn forward against the opponent's best available move? This depends on the first two: the move has to be worth doing and worth its price before its geometry matters. A cheap, high-value move into a spot that dies on the enemy's turn is still a bad move.

Step 4 — Concentration (Lesson 4)

Are you bringing enough to remove the target with margin, and not a scrap more? This sizes the play, and it depends on the space step naming what and where the target is. The target band's floor ("is anything actually dying?") and ceiling ("sledgehammer on an ant?") both apply here, so you commit inside the band and redirect the surplus.

Step 5 — Timing (Lesson 5)

Now that you have a sized, positioned, priced, worthwhile move, do you have to commit it now, or can you learn more first? This fires the conclusion the other four built. It comes last because there is nothing to time until the decision exists. Sequence the turn so this move lands on the last moment it's still fully effective.

Why this matters

Two things make the chain more than a mnemonic.

The order is a dependency graph, not a preference. Each step consumes the previous step's output. Value produces a candidate move; cost prices that move; space places it; concentration sizes it; timing releases it. Skip a step and the later ones have nothing to act on: sizing a target you haven't chosen, or timing a decision you haven't made, is incoherent. This is why "drop to the top and walk down" works as a rescue when a turn feels tangled — the first question you stall on is the real problem, and everything below it was noise.

The specifics are regenerable, so you don't have to memorise them. Every concrete tactic — screen the charge lane, trade a cheap unit for a turn of tempo, hold the midfield objective, focus one target down, wait for the shooting before charging — is one of these five firing in a specific board state. That means you can regenerate the right tactic from the chain instead of recalling a list of rules of thumb. Memorise the five questions and their order, and the tactics fall out of the position in front of you.

The Art of War Running the chain before you commit is what Sun Tzu meant by winning before fighting: "the victorious strategist only seeks battle after the victory has been won, whereas he who is destined to defeat first fights and afterwards looks for victory."1 (Ch. IV, Tactical Dispositions.) The chain is how you win first — you settle value, cost, space, mass, and timing in your head, and the dice merely confirm a result the sequence already decided.

The trap The shallow read treats the five as an unordered checklist, any-order, tick them off. The deep read sees a dependency chain: value gates cost gates space gates concentration gates timing, each feeding the next. The near-miss is running them in the wrong order — sizing or timing a move before value has confirmed it's worth doing at all — which produces a beautifully-executed play toward a goal that never mattered. The order is the content.

Recall — answer out loud before peeking:

① State the five steps of the chain, in order.

show answer

Value → cost → space → concentration → timing. What moves the scoreboard; could I do it cheaper; what do I control/expose one turn forward; enough to remove with margin and no more; commit now or learn more first.

② Why must value come before cost?

show answer

Cost prices a move's exchange rate, and you can only ask "the same effect for less" once value has told you what effect you're buying. Pricing a move you haven't confirmed is worth doing is incoherent.

③ You're tangled mid-turn and don't know what to do. What's the recovery procedure?

show answer

Drop to the top of the chain and walk down. The first question you can't answer cleanly is the one that's actually stuck; everything below it was noise.

④ Why does the course claim you don't have to memorise specific tactics?

show answer

Because every specific tactic is one of the five principles firing in a particular board state. Memorise the five questions and their order and the right tactic regenerates from the position in front of you.
