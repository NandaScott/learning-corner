Wargaming Fundamentals · Lesson 5

Commitment under uncertainty

The first four principles each hand you a conclusion: what's worth doing, what it costs, where to stand, how much to bring. This one is different in kind. It doesn't tell you what to do; it tells you when to do it. A correct conclusion executed at the wrong moment is still a blunder, and this is the principle that keeps the timing from wrecking the plan.

The concept

Information arrives over the course of a turn. You know the least at the very start, before anything has moved or been rolled, and you know more with every action that resolves. So every decision you can defer is one you will get to make with more information than you have now. The discipline is to sequence your own turn so that information-revealing actions happen first and irreversible commitments happen last. Commit when you are guessing least.

Deferring has teeth because an uncommitted unit is a superposition of threats. Until it acts, it threatens several places at once, and the opponent has to account for all of them. The moment it commits, it collapses into one fixed result and the other threats evaporate. Holding it uncommitted keeps the opponent guessing and drains their certainty; spending it early hands that certainty back for free.

The steps

Five moves, from why to wait to when to stop waiting:

  1. this principle = TIMING           the other four give conclusions; this fires them
  2. defer decisions                   information arrives through the turn; know most at the end
  3. why deferral has teeth            an uncommitted unit = a superposition of threats
  4. but deferral costs tempo          land on the LAST moment it's still fully effective
  5. two dials + reversibility         weight by worst case; tune variance to the scoreboard

Steps 2–3 are the case for waiting. Steps 4–5 stop waiting from becoming its own mistake.

Step 1 — This principle is about timing, not choice

The other four principles resolve what the right move is. This one governs the instant you act on it. That makes it the capstone: it sits on top of the others and decides when their conclusions get fired. A perfectly-sized attack (Lesson 4) against the right target for a good trade (Lesson 2) can still be a mistake if you throw it on the first activation when waiting three activations would have told you whether it was even needed.

Step 2 — Defer what you can, because information arrives through the turn

Sequence your turn so the actions that reveal information go first and the irreversible commitments go last. Move the scouts before the hammer. Shoot the unit whose result changes your next decision before you commit the unit that depends on it. Every activation you spend teaches you something about the board, and a decision made later in the turn is made against a board you can actually see rather than one you're guessing at.

Step 3 — Why deferral has teeth: the superposition of threats

An uncommitted unit is worth more than its datasheet, because it threatens several places at once.

  uncommitted:   unit ──▶ threatens A
                      ├──▶ threatens B      opponent must cover all three
                      └──▶ threatens C

  committed to A:  unit ──▶ A               B and C are now free for them

While the unit is uncommitted the opponent has to respect every option; the instant it commits, the superposition collapses to one and the others vanish. That optionality is the resource you spend when you commit early.

Step 4 — But deferral costs tempo, so land on the last effective moment

Waiting is not free. Windows close, the opponent acts on their turn, and a commitment held too long can miss its moment entirely. So the skill is not "always wait." It is landing on the last moment a commitment is still fully effective: not earlier, where you'd be guessing needlessly, and not later, where the window has already shut. Too early spends optionality you didn't need to spend; too late means the option expired in your hand.

Step 5 — Two dials, weighted by reversibility

Two adjustments sit on top. First, when the stakes are high, weight a commitment by its worst case, not its average: prefer a survivable floor over a prettier average outcome. Second, tune your appetite for variance to the scoreboard. Behind, take swingy, high-ceiling plays, because the safe line just loses slowly. Ahead, take low-floor plays that protect the lead even at the cost of upside. And weight all of it by reversibility: a cheap, takeable-back move can go early on partial information, while an irreversible one you defer until you're forced or nearly certain.

Why this matters

Two things carry forward.

Timing is where correct plans go to die. The other four principles can all be right and the turn still lost, because the right action fired on the wrong activation stops being the right action. This is why the principle is the capstone rather than a sixth item in a list: it is the one that decides whether the other four ever get expressed cleanly. Sequencing the turn is not housekeeping; it is the plan.

The scoreboard changes what "good" even means. The same swingy play is correct when you're behind and wrong when you're ahead, because a lead should be protected with low-floor lines and a deficit demands high-ceiling ones. This closes the loop back to Lesson 1: beyond telling you whether you're winning, the score-difference tells you which risks to take. Reading the scoreboard is how you set your variance appetite, and the appetite is how you pick between plays the first four principles both endorse.

The Art of War Sun Tzu's Chapter V, Energy, is this exact image: force as stored potential released at the right instant. "Energy may be likened to the bending of a crossbow; decision, to the releasing of a trigger."1 The build-up and the release are separate acts, and the release is timed. His momentum is the same: "the energy developed by good fighting men is as the momentum of a round stone rolled down a mountain thousands of feet in height,"1 a potential held until it is let go. And on the timing itself: "The quality of decision is like the well-timed swoop of a falcon which enables it to strike and destroy its victim."1 Build the potential, hold it, release it late and decisively.
Key line "The other four principles give conclusions; this one times the trigger. Information arrives through the turn, so defer what you can and sequence information-first, irreversible-last. An uncommitted unit is a superposition of threats that drains the opponent's certainty. But deferral costs tempo, so land on the last moment a commitment is still fully effective. Weight high-stakes calls by their worst case, tune variance to the scoreboard, and defer the irreversible ones longest."
The trap Two shallow reads pull in opposite directions. One is "commit early and dictate the game," which spends optionality you never needed to spend and collapses your threats for free. The other is "always keep your options open," which lets windows close and dies waiting. The deep answer is neither: commit at the last moment the action is still fully effective, and set that moment by reversibility: reversible moves can go early, irreversible ones wait. The extra check most people miss is whether the opponent can take the option off the table before your next turn; if they can, "later" may already be gone.
Heuristic — the timing question "Do I have to commit this now, or can I learn more first?" If waiting doesn't cost the window, wait. The bounding clause, said in the same breath: "but the moment this is the last turn it's still effective, commit hard." And check not only whether the option expires on its own, but whether the opponent can remove it before you act again.

Recall — answer out loud before peeking:

① Why sequence a turn so information-revealing actions go first and irreversible ones last?

show answer

You know the least at the start of the turn and more with every action that resolves. Deferring an irreversible commit means making it against a board you can see, guessing least.

② What makes an uncommitted unit worth more than a committed one?

show answer

It's a superposition of threats, it threatens several places at once and the opponent must cover all of them. Committing collapses it to one result and frees the others. Optionality is the resource you spend by committing early.

③ You're comfortably ahead on the scoreboard. Swingy high-ceiling play, or low-floor safe play, and why?

show answer

Low-floor. Ahead, protect the lead even at the cost of upside; the swingy play only adds risk you don't need. Behind, it flips: the safe line just loses slowly, so you want the high ceiling. Variance appetite tracks the score-difference.

④ Deferring is powerful, so why not always wait as long as possible?

show answer

Deferral costs tempo and windows close. Land on the last moment the commitment is still fully effective; too late and the option expired in your hand. Also check whether the opponent can remove the option before your next turn.
