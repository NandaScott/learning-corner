Wargaming Fundamentals · Lesson 7

Two cross-cutting skills

The five principles and the chain assume two things: that you can read what the opponent will do, and that you can trust your own reasoning about the board. Both assumptions are shakier than they look. This lesson covers the two habits that hold them up, how to model the opponent, and how to catch yourself lying to yourself.

The concept

These two skills aren't a sixth and seventh principle. They cut across all five, because every step of the chain depends on reading the opponent honestly and reasoning about your own plan honestly. The first skill fixes how you model the other player: by capability, not psychology. The second fixes how you audit your own plan: distrust the one you can most easily justify. One points outward, one points inward, and both exist to stop you from believing a comfortable story instead of reading the board.

The two skills

  SKILL 1 — outward:  model the opponent by CAPABILITY, not psychology
              map what they CAN do (threat ranges are visible)
              assume their best move TOWARD THE MISSION
              safe vs their best play  =  safe vs every worse one

  SKILL 2 — inward:   distrust the plan you can most easily JUSTIFY
              reasoning is a lawyer for what you already wanted
              the blunt heuristics win because they're too simple to argue with
              the smoother the justification, the harder you check

Skill 1 keeps your read of the enemy honest; skill 2 keeps your read of yourself honest. The chain only works if both are.

Skill 1 — Model the opponent by capability, not psychology

Do not try to predict the specific thing the opponent will do. Map what they can do: their threat ranges are visible on the table (Lesson 3), so their capabilities are legible in a way their intentions never are. Then assume they will make their best available move, best toward the mission, not the most violent or the most spectacular. Position so that you are fine against that best move, because being safe against their strongest play means you are automatically safe against every weaker one.

  cover their BEST move  ─────▶  automatically covered against all worse moves
  cover a GUESSED move   ─────▶  exposed the moment they do something better

A rational opponent is legible through the position they're in, because they're solving the same scoring problem you are. Their personality shrinks to a tiebreaker among the threats you couldn't fully cover.

This is why "what will they do?" is the wrong question and "what's the best thing they could do?" is the right one. The first invites you to guess, and guesses about intent are where you get punished. The second is answerable from the board, and defending against it is strictly safer.

Skill 2 — Distrust the plan you can most easily justify

Your reasoning is a lawyer, not a judge. It will build a persuasive case for whatever you already wanted to do, which is usually the exciting kill rather than the boring objective grab. The more fluent and one-sided the justification feels, the more suspicious you should be, because fluency is a sign you're rationalising a decision already made, not evaluating options.

This is exactly why the course's heuristics are kept blunt. "If this doesn't move the score, what am I doing?" and "sledgehammer to kill an ant?" and "is anything actually dying?" work because they are too simple to argue your way around. A blunt question you can't lawyer is worth more than a subtle one you can. When a plan feels obviously right and the justification comes too easily, run the blunt heuristics against it on purpose.

Why this matters

Two things carry forward.

Capability-modelling collapses the hardest part of the game into something readable. Predicting a specific human is nearly impossible; reading threat ranges and assuming the best mission-move is routine. By modelling capability you convert an unknowable psychology problem into the same spatial reading you already do in Lesson 3, and you get a defensive guarantee for free: safe against their best is safe against all. The opponent's cleverness stops being a threat you fear and becomes a constraint you plan around.

Self-distrust is what keeps the other principles from being decoration. Every principle in this course can be recited and then ignored in the moment, because the lawyer in your head will explain why this exciting play is the exception. The habit of distrusting your smoothest justification is the enforcement mechanism for the chain. Without it, the principles become things you know rather than things you do.

The Art of War On modelling by capability, Sun Tzu is explicit: "The art of war teaches us to rely not on the likelihood of the enemy's not coming, but on our own readiness to receive him; not on the chance of his not attacking, but rather on the fact that we have made our position unassailable."1 (Ch. VIII.) And his most famous line is the same instruction: "If you know the enemy and know yourself, you need not fear the result of a hundred battles."2 (Ch. III.) On self-deception, the opening of the whole work: "All warfare is based on deception,"3 (Ch. I), and the first person you deceive is yourself.
Key line "Two habits cut across all five principles. Model the opponent by capability, not psychology: map what they can do from their visible threat ranges, assume their best move toward the mission, and know that safe against their best is safe against every worse. And distrust the plan you can most easily justify: reasoning is a lawyer for what you already wanted, usually the exciting kill, so the blunt heuristics win precisely because they're too simple to argue with."
The trap For skill 1, the near-miss is modelling the opponent's likely or most aggressive move instead of their best move toward the mission: you defend the wrong thing and get scored on while you're braced for a fight that was never their best line. For skill 2, the trap is trusting a plan because it's easy to justify; ease of justification is evidence you're rationalising, not evidence the plan is good. The deep answer treats a fluent, one-sided case as a warning sign, not a green light.
Heuristic — one per skill Outward: "What's the best thing they could do here, and am I fine against it?", not "what will they do?" Inward: "This feels obviously right, so run the blunt heuristics against it anyway." The smoother the story, the more it needs the check.

Recall — answer out loud before peeking:

① Why model the opponent by what they can do rather than what they will do?

show answer

Capabilities are visible on the table (threat ranges); intentions aren't. Guessing intent is where you get punished. Reading capability turns an unknowable psychology problem into a readable spatial one.

② Why is being safe against the opponent's best move enough?

show answer

Because safe against their strongest play is automatically safe against every weaker one. Cover a guessed move instead and you're exposed the instant they do something better.

③ "Best available move" — does that mean their most damaging attack?

show answer

No. Best toward the mission, the move that most helps them win on the scoreboard, which is often an objective play, not the most violent one. Model the mission-optimal move, not the flashiest.

④ Why does the course rely on blunt heuristics instead of subtle ones?

show answer

Because reasoning is a lawyer for what you already wanted. Blunt heuristics work precisely because they're too simple to argue around, so they fire even when a fluent justification for the exciting play is staring you in the face.

Primary source — read this one Read Sun Tzu's Chapter III, Attack by Stratagem (for "know the enemy and know yourself") and Chapter VIII, Variation in Tactics (for "rely on your own readiness"), in the Giles translation (Project Gutenberg #132) or the MIT Internet Classics Archive. Together they are the ancient form of both skills: read the enemy by their capability and your own readiness, and beware the deception that starts with yourself.
