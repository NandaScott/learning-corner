Wargaming Fundamentals · Lesson 1

Value flows from the win condition

This is the axiom the rest of the course rests on. It assigns value to every decision on the table: get it wrong and every later calculation is careful arithmetic toward the wrong answer. So before anything else, the first question is what is actually worth doing, and why.

The concept

A wargame is an optimisation problem with a single defined metric. In Warhammer 40,000 that metric is the mission score (Victory Points), not the body count.1 Everything on the table has value only insofar as it moves that score: yours up, or the opponent's down. Killing is never valuable in itself, only when it protects your scoring or denies theirs.

It sounds obvious, and it gets violated constantly, because the game feels like it is about destroying the enemy army. That pull toward the fight produces the most common losing pattern at every table:

          "I killed more and still lost."

That's not bad luck. It's optimising the wrong metric: playing the fight instead of the mission.

Why is 40K actually built this way? Because the rules make the win condition explicit and make body count almost irrelevant to it. In 11th edition you win on Victory Points, earned mostly by controlling the mission's terrain objectives. You control one by having the higher total Objective Control (OC) of models in range, and the game re-checks that control continuously, at the end of each phase and turn.1 How much that control is worth, and when, is set by the mission (in 11th, the primary mission is chosen by the two players' force dispositions); only a handful of secondary missions reward killing at all. A wiped-out enemy army sitting on zero objectives while yours holds three is a losing board.

The steps — deriving the axiom and its two corollaries

The principle is one axiom plus two consequences that follow from how the score is kept:

  AXIOM        value(action) = how much it moves the SCORE-DIFFERENCE
               (my VP − their VP), and nothing else

     │  because the score is a difference of two totals…
     ▼
  COROLLARY 1  deny a point  ==  score a point      (same currency)
               choose whichever is cheaper right now

     │  because the mission scores every round & they're proactive…
     ▼
  COROLLARY 2  no "doing nothing" while ahead        (a lead is a race,
               a lead must be actively defended       not a bank balance)

Everything below unpacks these three lines. It's the root the rest of the course hangs off.

Step 1 — The axiom: value is movement of the score-difference

The only number that decides a game of 40K is your VP minus the opponent's, the score-difference. Not your total on its own, not the model count. So an action's value is just how much it moves that difference in your favour. A charge that wipes a full unit but moves the score-difference by zero has produced zero value. This is the axiom because it lets you compare a shooting attack, a screen, and an objective grab on the same scale: they're all candidate moves of one number.

Step 2 — Corollary 1: denying a point is worth exactly as much as scoring one

Because the game is decided by the difference of two totals, "increase mine by one" and "decrease theirs by one" change that difference identically. They are the same currency. This is arithmetic, not a slogan: (M+1) − T and M − (T−1) are the same number. So the choice between scoring and denying is never about which feels like progress; it's about which is cheaper to achieve right now. Often, contesting the objective the opponent needs, parking one cheap unit on it, is far cheaper than grinding out a point of your own, and it moves the difference just as far.

Flag — "deny = score" is a rule fact, not a vibe This equivalence holds because the win condition is a difference of two totals. In a hypothetical game scored only on your own total, denial would be worthless. Always ground the claim in "the difference decides": that is what makes offence and defence the same currency here.

Step 3 — Corollary 2: there is no "doing nothing" while ahead

Objective control is re-contested every round, the rules recompute who holds each terrain objective continuously, and the opponent is always proactive: they get a full turn to move the difference back. So a lead is not a stored balance you can sit on; it is a snapshot of a race that never pauses. "Holding" a lead is active work: protecting your scoring trajectory and denying theirs, turn after turn. The moment you coast and treat the lead as banked, you hand the initiative back, and the opponent closes the difference on their turn while you do nothing on yours.

Why this matters

Three consequences follow, and they set up the rest of the course.

1. It is the foundation the rest of the course builds on. Every principle still to come is machinery for moving the score-difference efficiently, so each one only makes sense once you know that the score-difference is the thing being moved. Name what you're optimising before you study how to optimise it. That's why this is Lesson 1.

2. The tempting move and the valuable move are usually different. The board keeps offering a satisfying kill that moves the difference by nothing, while the point-winning move is some unglamorous unit standing on an objective doing "nothing." The discipline of Principle 1 is noticing that gap in the moment, which is why the heuristic below is kept blunt enough to fire when a tempting target is in front of you.

3. Combat mostly confirms a result positioning already decided. This is the bridge to Sun Tzu: if value lives in the mission, the fight is downstream of the positioning that secured it. You win by being in the right place relative to the objectives; the shooting usually just confirms it.

The Art of War Sun Tzu's most-quoted line is this same axiom: "Hence to fight and conquer in all your battles is not supreme excellence; supreme excellence consists in breaking the enemy's resistance without fighting."2 (Ch. III, Attack by Stratagem.) Fighting is a cost, not a goal: the aim is the objective the war exists to secure, and battle is one expensive, often avoidable tool toward it. And on positioning deciding the result before combat: "the victorious strategist only seeks battle after the victory has been won, whereas he who is destined to defeat first fights and afterwards looks for victory."2 (Ch. IV, Tactical Dispositions.) Secure your position relative to the objective before the shooting; the shooting confirms what position already decided.
Key line "A wargame is an optimisation problem with one metric: in 40K, the mission score, not the body count. Every action's value is exactly how much it moves the score-difference. So denying a point equals scoring one (only the difference decides), and a lead is a race that never pauses, not a balance you can sit on. Killing is instrumental: it matters only when it protects your scoring or denies theirs."
The trap The shallow answer treats the goal as "destroy the enemy army" and objectives as a tiebreaker. The deep answer inverts it: the mission is the goal and killing is instrumental, valuable only insofar as it moves the score-difference. The tell of a shallow player is "I killed more and still lost" said as if it were unlucky rather than a category error. Do not overcorrect into "killing is worthless" either: it is instrumental, not worthless.
Heuristic — keep it blunt on purpose "If this action doesn't increase my score or suppress theirs, what the hell am I doing?" Blunt so it still fires when a tempting kill is staring you in the face. It's the first question to ask of any move you're considering.

Recall — answer out loud before peeking:

① Your army is nearly wiped, but you hold 3 terrain objectives to their 1 going into the last scoring. Winning or losing?

show answer

Winning (on primary). The win condition is the mission score, controlling terrain objectives via Objective Control, not models on the table. Body count has no intrinsic value; a decimated army holding the objectives is a winning board.

② Why is denying the opponent a point worth exactly as much as scoring one?

show answer

Because the game is decided by the difference of the two VP totals. "Increase mine by one" and "decrease theirs by one" move that difference identically, same currency. You choose between them by which is cheaper right now.

③ You're 10 VP ahead at the start of your turn. Is "play safe and do nothing" a valid plan?

show answer

No. Objective control is re-contested every round and the opponent is proactive, so a lead is a snapshot of a race that never pauses, not a stored balance. Coasting hands back the initiative; holding a lead is active work (protect your trajectory, deny theirs).

④ A shooting attack kills a whole enemy unit, but that unit wasn't going to contest any objective and its death doesn't change how the game scores. What's the value of that attack, and why?

show answer

Zero (or near it). Value is movement of the score-difference. A kill that neither protects your scoring nor denies theirs hasn't moved that number, so it produced no value, however satisfying it looked.
