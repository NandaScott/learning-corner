Wargaming Fundamentals · Lesson 3

Threat range and the control of space

Lesson 2 spent board space as a resource without ever saying what it is. This lesson says what it is. Space on the table is not the ground your models physically stand on; it is the ground they can reach and affect. Once you see the board that way, positioning stops being decoration and becomes the thing that decides which fights happen and where the scoring is safe.

The concept

The atom of board space is the threat range: a unit's movement plus its reach, where reach is its gun range or its charge distance. Added together, those define the whole area the unit can affect this turn. A unit with 6" movement and a 24" gun projects a 30" threat range in every direction it can turn to face. That circle, not the base the model sits on, is the space it occupies in any sense that matters.

The part that takes a moment to accept: a unit controls its entire threat range whether or not it ever fires a shot. It controls that space because a rational opponent has to respect the footprint. Walk a unit into that circle and it can be shot or charged, so the opponent routes around it, delays, or commits something to deal with it first. The threat does the work. The trigger being pulled is almost incidental to the control the threat already exerted.

The steps

Five moves take you from a single unit's reach to reading the whole board:

  1. threat range   = movement + reach (gun range or charge)   the area a unit can affect
  2. control        comes from the threat, not the shot         they must respect the footprint
  3. the board      = overlapping threat ranges                 yours / theirs / contested
  4. terrain + LoS  break the symmetry                          threaten without being threatened
  5. read it        one turn FORWARD, after they move           safe-now is not safe

Steps 1–3 build the map. Steps 4–5 are what separate a real reading of it from a naive one.

Step 1 — Threat range is movement plus reach

Reach alone is not the threat; a 24" gun on a unit that can also move 6" threatens 30". This is why the threat range is measured from where the unit could be at the end of its move, not from where it stands now. For a melee unit, reach is the charge distance, so its threat range is movement plus charge range. Every unit projects one of these, and its size is the first fact you should know about any model on the table.

Step 2 — Control comes from the threat, not the shot

Because the opponent must respect the footprint, the space inside a threat range is effectively yours to police even on turns you never fire. A gunline that never shoots still forbids the ground in front of it, because walking into it is punished. This is the cheapest control there is: the unit does its job by existing in the right place, and the shooting, if it comes, is a bonus on top of the control already exerted.

Step 3 — The board is a map of overlapping threat ranges

Lay every unit's threat range over the table and it partitions into three kinds of ground.

     space only YOU threaten          space only THEY threaten
   ┌────────────────────────┐      ┌────────────────────────┐
   │        YOURS            │██████│        THEIRS          │
   │  (safe to operate in)   │██████│  (they operate freely) │
   └────────────────────────┘██████└────────────────────────┘
                              ▲
                         CONTESTED
             both threaten it — this is where fights happen,
             and where the objectives are won or lost

Ground only you threaten is effectively yours; ground only they threaten is theirs; the overlap is contested. Objectives sit in contested space by design, which is why controlling the space around a point is how you protect the scoring from Lesson 1.

Good positioning, in one line: maximise the space you threaten while minimising the space that threatens you.

Step 4 — Terrain and line of sight break the symmetry

So far the map is symmetric: if you threaten them, they threaten you. Terrain breaks that. A unit behind a blocker sits inside the enemy's threat range but is not actually threatened, because they cannot draw line of sight to it. That is how you threaten without being threatened: put your unit where its reach covers the ground but a piece of terrain denies the return fire. Line of sight, not raw distance, is what makes a threat real, so terrain is the lever that bends the symmetric map in your favour.

Step 5 — Read it one turn forward, after they move

The game is turn-based, not continuous, so the enemy's threat ranges are not fixed where they sit now. On their turn they move first, then act, which means every enemy unit's real threat range is its footprint after a full move. The safety of a position has to be read against that projected picture, not the current one.

  reading NOW (naive):        enemy ●·······| reach        spot ✓ looks safe
  reading FORWARD (correct):  enemy →→→move→→→ ●·······| reach   spot ✗ now covered

Project each enemy unit its full movement, then ask what it reaches. A spot that is safe from their current positions but open once they advance is not a safe spot; it is a trap that springs on their turn.

Why this matters

Two consequences carry the weight here.

Control is the cheapest thing you can buy. A threat range works without firing, so occupying the right ground costs you nothing but the unit's position, and it still forces the opponent to react. In the terms from Lesson 2, that is an excellent exchange rate: you spend a placement and buy control of a whole region. The player who thinks in threat ranges is constantly getting this discount; the player who thinks only in shots keeps paying full price for effects that good positioning would have given free.

The board decides where the fights are before anyone shoots. Contested space is where combat happens, and contested space is set by how the threat ranges overlap, which you control by where you place and move. Reading one turn forward is how you make sure the contested ground is ground you chose. A position that ignores the forward read hands that choice to the opponent, and the objectives from Lesson 1 sit right in the middle of it.

The Art of War Sun Tzu's Chapter VI, Weak Points and Strong, is this principle almost line for line. Control of space is his opening claim: "the clever combatant imposes his will on the enemy, but does not allow the enemy's will to be imposed on him."1 On taking ground first and making them come to you inside your threat range: "Whoever is first in the field and awaits the coming of the enemy, will be fresh for the fight; whoever is second in the field and has to hasten to battle will arrive exhausted."1 He tells you to "appear at points which the enemy must hasten to defend."1 And on letting terrain dictate the shape of your plan: "Water shapes its course according to the nature of the ground over which it flows... just as water retains no constant shape, so in warfare there are no constant conditions."1
Key line "The atom of space is the threat range: movement plus reach, the whole area a unit can affect. A unit controls that range whether or not it fires, because the opponent has to respect it, so the board is a map of overlapping threat ranges: yours, theirs, and the contested overlap where fights happen and objectives are won. Terrain breaks the symmetry by letting you threaten without being threatened, and because the game is turn-based you read it one turn forward, against where they can be after they move."
The trap Two shallow reads. The first is "threat range means gun range," forgetting that movement is half of it, so the real footprint is far larger than the datasheet number. The second, and the one that loses games, is judging a position safe because nothing threatens it right now. Safety has to be read one turn forward, against each enemy unit projected its full movement. A spot safe from their current positions but open after they advance is a trap, and the deep answer always checks the post-move picture.
Heuristic — the two questions Defensive: "What can shoot this, where I'm putting it, after they move?" Actually name the enemy units whose projected threat range covers the spot before you commit the model. Offensive, once the defensive check is automatic: "Am I making them react to my threat, or reacting to theirs?"

Recall — answer out loud before peeking:

① What two quantities add together to make a unit's threat range?

show answer

Movement plus reach, where reach is gun range for shooting or charge distance for melee. A 6" move with a 24" gun threatens 30", measured from where the unit could end its move.

② Does a unit have to fire to control its threat range? Why?

show answer

No. A rational opponent must respect the footprint, so walking into it is punished and they route around, delay, or commit to clearing it. The threat controls the space; the shot is almost incidental.

③ A spot is out of range of every enemy unit's current position. Is it safe to place a unit there?

show answer

Not necessarily. Read one turn forward: project each enemy unit its full movement and ask what it then reaches. Safe from their current positions but open after they advance is a trap, not a safe spot.

④ How does terrain let you threaten a unit without being threatened back?

show answer

Line of sight, not distance, makes a threat real. Put your unit where its reach covers the ground but a blocker denies the enemy a line back to it: you sit inside their threat range yet are not actually threatened.

Primary source — read this one Read Sun Tzu's Chapter VI, Weak Points and Strong, in the Giles translation (Project Gutenberg #132) or the MIT Internet Classics Archive. It is the clearest ancient statement of control of space: imposing your will rather than submitting to theirs, taking ground first, appearing where the enemy is forced to respond, and letting the terrain shape the plan. Read it as a description of overlapping threat ranges and the mapping is almost exact.
