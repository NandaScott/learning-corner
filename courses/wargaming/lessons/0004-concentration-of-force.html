Wargaming Fundamentals · Lesson 4

Concentration of force

Lesson 3 told you where force will meet force. Lessons 2 and 3 both stopped short of one question on purpose: how much to bring there. This lesson answers it. The short version is that you bring more than the enemy can answer at that one spot, even if it means being weak everywhere else, and the reason why runs deeper than "focus fire is good."

The concept

Concentration of force means bringing more strength to bear at the decisive point than the enemy can answer there. Not more strength overall, more strength locally, at the moment it matters. You can be outnumbered across the whole table and still be the stronger side at the one place the game turns on, and that local edge is what decides it.

This works because combat output is nonlinear. A unit does close to 100% of its job right up until it dies, then it does zero. There is a cliff between those two states, and only removal crosses it. Spreading damage across three units leaves three near-full threats still shooting back; the same damage poured into one unit removes it, and takes a third of the enemy's output off the table for good. A dead unit can't shoot back. A wounded one shoots back almost as hard as a healthy one.

The steps

Five moves, from why concentration works to how much to commit:

  1. concentration = LOCAL superiority        more than they can answer HERE, not overall
  2. why it works  = the removal cliff         ~100% output until dead, then 0
  3. so:  removal beats chipping               kill one, or bloody three?  kill one
  4. the price     = thin elsewhere            can't be strong everywhere — that's the mechanism
  5. how much      = the target band           enough to remove with margin, not a scrap more

Steps 1–3 are why. Steps 4–5 are the discipline: what concentration costs, and how to size it.

Step 1 — Concentration is local superiority

The aim is to be many against few at the point of contact, not across the whole army list. You reach it two ways. Across space, mass your whole against a fraction of theirs by striking where they cannot reinforce in time. Across time, collapse a unit before it gets to act, which removes its output and steals the activation it would have spent. Both produce the same thing: your full strength meeting a part of theirs.

Step 2 — Why it works: the removal cliff

The load-bearing fact is that a unit's effectiveness does not degrade smoothly with damage.

  effectiveness
     100% ●━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━┓   a wounded unit still shoots from up here
                               ┃
                               ┃   ← the removal cliff (one wound wide)
       0%                      ┗●━━━━━━  dead: output gone, and gone permanently

   spread damage over 3 units → 3 units still near 100%, all still shooting
   pour the same into 1 unit  → 1 unit removed, its output subtracted for good

Damage only pays off when it crosses the cliff. Everything short of removal leaves the target doing almost its full job.

Step 3 — So removal beats chipping

Given the cliff, the choice between killing one unit and bloodying three is not close. Three bloodied units keep shooting at nearly full effect next turn; one dead unit is a permanent subtraction from the enemy's output. Concentration is just the decision to always take the permanent subtraction. It is why "spread the love" across enemy targets is usually the wrong instinct, however even it looks on the damage tally.

Step 4 — The price: strong here means thin there

Concentration has a cost, and the cost is the mechanism, not a flaw in it. You cannot be strong everywhere, so massing at the decisive point requires being deliberately thin somewhere else. That is the correct trade, not a mistake to feel bad about. The failure is the opposite one: trying to be adequate at every point, which makes you strong at none and hands the enemy local superiority wherever they choose to concentrate.

Step 5 — How much: the target band

Sizing the commitment is a margin problem, not an exact sum. Commit force to a target until your rough estimate comfortably exceeds its wounds, then hard-stop and send the surplus to the next target. Comfortably, not on the nose: "exactly enough on average" fails half the time, because half your rolls come in below average. This gives a band with two edges, and you have already met the upper one.

  under-commit  ┤        TARGET BAND        ├  over-commit
  relying on an   enough to remove WITH margin   still feeding a target
  above-average   and then not a scrap more      already dead in expectation
  roll to finish

  floor  →  Principle 4:  "is anything actually dying this turn?"
  ceiling → Principle 2:  "am I using a sledgehammer to kill an ant?"

The ceiling is Lesson 2's overpaying question; the floor is this lesson's. Together they define how much force a task deserves: enough to remove it with margin, and no more. Required margin scales with the stakes — pay for certainty on a must-die target, pay the minimum on a trivial one.

Why this matters

Two things carry forward.

The two mistakes have opposite signatures, and you can name each. Relying on an above-average roll to finish a kill is the tell of under-commitment: you sized to the average and the average isn't enough half the time. Still pouring fire into a target already dead in expectation is over-commitment: that surplus was a whole other unit you could have removed. Naming which one you're about to make is usually enough to stop it.

Concentration and the exchange rate are the same discipline from two ends. Lesson 2 capped the top of a commitment; this lesson sets the floor and explains why the floor exists. A plan that leaves every enemy unit alive but bloodied has dispersed, which is both a concentration failure and a bad exchange rate, since you spent real resources and bought no permanent removal. When the two principles agree that a commitment is wrong, it is wrong twice over.

The Art of War Sun Tzu builds much of his art on this. He wants you to mass while they scatter: "We can form a single united body, while the enemy must split up into fractions. Hence there will be a whole pitted against separate parts of a whole, which means that we shall be many to the enemy's few."1 He reaches local superiority through the observation that a defender cannot be strong everywhere: "should the enemy strengthen his van, he will weaken his rear... If he sends reinforcements everywhere, he will everywhere be weak."1 And he quantifies the instinct in his force ratios: "if our forces are ten to the enemy's one, to surround him; if five to one, to attack him."2

Key line "Concentration is local superiority: more than the enemy can answer at the decisive point, not more overall. It works because combat output is nonlinear — a unit does full damage until it dies, then none, so removal is permanent and chipping is nearly free for the enemy. The price is being thin elsewhere, which is the mechanism, not a flaw. Size it as a margin: commit until you comfortably exceed the target's wounds, then hard-stop and redirect the surplus."
The trap The shallow read is "concentrate = focus fire," true but shallow, and it misses both edges. The near-miss that costs games is sizing a kill to the average damage: it sounds disciplined, but average means you fall short half the time, and a target left alive on a below-average roll keeps its full output. The deep answer commits to comfortably exceed the wounds, scales that margin to how much the kill matters, and then stops — because the surplus past "dead in expectation" is another unit you could have removed.
Heuristic — the floor on force "Is anything actually dying this turn?" If the plan leaves everything alive but bloodied, you dispersed. This sets the lower edge of the band; paired with Lesson 2's sledgehammer question at the upper edge, the two bracket how much force a target deserves.

Recall — answer out loud before peeking:

① Why does removing one unit beat bloodying three, even when the total damage dealt is the same?

show answer

Combat output is nonlinear: a unit does ~100% of its job until it dies, then zero. Three bloodied units still shoot at nearly full effect; one dead unit is a permanent subtraction. Only removal crosses the cliff.

② You size a shooting attack to the target's average expected wounds. What's wrong with that?

show answer

Average means you fall short half the time, and a target left alive keeps its full output. Commit to comfortably exceed its wounds, with the margin scaled to how much the kill matters.

③ Concentrating at the decisive point leaves you thin elsewhere. Mistake or mechanism?

show answer

Mechanism. You can't be strong everywhere, so massing here requires being thin there. The real mistake is trying to be adequate everywhere, which makes you strong nowhere.

④ Name the two edges of the target band and which principle owns each.

show answer

Floor: "is anything actually dying?" (Principle 4, this lesson). Ceiling: "sledgehammer to kill an ant?" (Principle 2, Lesson 2). Enough to remove with margin, not a scrap more.
