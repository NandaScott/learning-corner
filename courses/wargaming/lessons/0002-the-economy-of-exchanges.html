Wargaming Fundamentals · Lesson 2

The economy of exchanges

Lesson 1 fixed the one thing worth optimising: the score-difference. This lesson asks the next question. Everything you do to move that number costs something, and the number never tells you whether you paid too much for it. So a move can be worth doing and still be a mistake, if there was a cheaper way to get the same result. That gap is where a lot of games are quietly lost.

The concept

Every action in a game of 40K spends a resource. Models, points, Command Points, activations, board space, and above all time: a fixed number of battle rounds, spent whether you use them well or not, and never refunded. So every decision is a purchase, and the only question worth asking about a purchase is the exchange rate: what you paid versus what you got, measured the one way Lesson 1 gave us, in movement of the score-difference.

This reframes something players get wrong by instinct. The natural scorecard for a fight is casualties: did I kill more than I lost? But casualties are a cost you paid, not the thing you bought. A unit that dies can still have been a good purchase, and a unit that survives a flawless kill can still have been a bad one. The corpse count is not the measure. The movement of the score-difference, per resource spent, is.

The steps

The principle is a purchase, a way to price it, two ways to misprice it, and a spending rule:

  EVERY ACTION = a purchase
     spends: models · points · CP · activations · board space · TIME (never refunded)
        │
        ▼
  PRICE IT BY  exchange rate = score-difference gained  per  resource spent
        │       (not by whether your models survived)
        │
        ├─ misprice A — the corpse count:  a unit that DIES can still WIN its trade
        │               if what it bought (a turn, a position) beat what it cost
        │
        └─ misprice B — overpaying:  winning the fight but spending a premium
                        resource on a cheap problem  →  its true cost is what it
                        could have done elsewhere (opportunity cost)
        ▼
  SPENDING RULE: pay the minimum that gets the effect, guard the surplus

Both mispricings survive because the fight looks won in each. The scoreboard, and the resources you have left, tell a different story.

Step 1 — Every action is a purchase, and time is the one you can't refund

Models and Command Points are obviously spent. Less obvious is that doing nothing spends too, because the battle runs a fixed number of rounds and each one passes regardless. A unit that spends a turn walking to where it isn't needed has spent that turn as surely as if it died. Time is the resource with no reserve and no refund, which is why wasting it rarely feels like a cost until the game ends and you're a round short.

Step 2 — Price the purchase by its exchange rate, not by your casualties

The exchange rate is score-difference gained divided by resources spent. That is the only figure that matters, and it is measured in the currency from Lesson 1, not in models. Asking "did I come out ahead on kills?" answers a different question than "did that move the score-difference in my favour for what it cost?" Most of the time the second question is the one that decides the game, and the first is a distraction dressed up as a scorecard.

Step 3 — A unit that dies can still win its trade

Cheap models that die but absorb a turn of the opponent's effort, or hold a position for a round, have often bought more than they cost. Their death is the price, not the outcome. A five-model screen that dies to a unit that would otherwise have reached your scoring units has done its job perfectly. Judge it by what it bought, not by the fact that it's now off the table.

Step 4 — Overpaying: the losing trade hidden inside a won fight

The subtler failure is spending a premium resource on a cheap problem. A pristine, no-losses kill can still be a bad trade, because the true cost of any action includes its opportunity cost: the best thing those same resources could have done instead. If your best unit spends the turn deleting something a cheap unit could have handled, and is now out of position for the thing only it could do, you won the fight and lost the trade. "It worked" is not the bar. "Nothing better was going spare" is.

Step 5 — The discipline: pay the minimum, guard the surplus

Both mispricings point to one habit: spend the minimum resource that gets the effect you need, and keep the surplus for the moments that decide the game. This is a cap on how much a task deserves. It is not about being timid with your army; it is about not emptying a premium account to solve a problem the loose change would have covered.

Why this matters

Two things carry forward from here.

Losses stop being failures and start being prices. Once a trade is priced in score-difference, a dead unit is just a receipt for whatever it bought. That frees you to spend units deliberately, sacrificing cheap ones to protect the score, instead of playing to keep models alive as if survival were the goal. Survival was never the metric.

Winning the fight is not proof you traded well. The dangerous mistakes here don't feel like mistakes, because you won the combat that made them. Opportunity cost is invisible on the table: you can see the unit you killed, not the objective your best unit failed to take because it was busy. Pricing every action by its exchange rate is how you catch the trade that lost while the fight was winning.

The Art of War Sun Tzu's second chapter, Waging War, is almost entirely about the cost of fighting. His warning is blunt: "There is no instance of a country having benefited from prolonged warfare."1 Winning battle after battle while the exchange rate bankrupts you is exactly the outcome he means. And on getting more per resource spent, he prized foraging off the enemy above all: "One cartload of the enemy's provisions is equivalent to twenty of one's own, and likewise a single picul of his provender is equivalent to twenty from one's own store."1 Taking from the enemy pays twice, since you gain what they lose in a single stroke, which is why denying or capturing their resources is the best exchange rate on the board.
Key line "Every action is a purchase, and the only question is the exchange rate: score-difference gained per resource spent, not whether my models survived. A unit that dies can still win its trade, and a flawless kill can still be a bad one once you count opportunity cost. So pay the minimum that gets the effect and guard the surplus for where the game is decided."
The trap The shallow read scores a fight by the kill-to-loss ratio: I destroyed more points than I lost, so I'm ahead. The deep read prices it in score-difference and includes opportunity cost, so a won fight can still be a lost trade. The near-miss that catches people is "a profitable trade means my models survived" — survival is one input to the price, not the price itself. The unit that dies buying a turn traded well; the unit that lives after overkilling a screen it was wasted on traded badly.
Heuristic — the cap on force "Am I using a sledgehammer to kill an ant?" It polices overpaying: spending your best resource on a problem beneath it. It sets the ceiling on how much force a task deserves, so the surplus stays free for the decisions that actually turn on it.

Recall — answer out loud before peeking:

① A five-model screen dies without killing anything, but it cost the enemy's hammer unit a full turn of movement and kept it off your scoring units. Good trade or bad?

show answer

Good. Its death is the price, not the outcome. It bought a turn of delay and protected your scoring, which moves the score-difference more than the cheap models cost. A dying unit can still win its trade.

② Your best unit kills a weak enemy screen with no losses at all. Why might this still be a bad trade?

show answer

Opportunity cost. The true cost includes the best thing that unit could have done instead. If a cheap unit could have handled the screen and your premium unit is now out of position for the job only it could do, you won the fight and lost the trade.

③ In what units is an exchange rate measured?

show answer

Movement of the score-difference per resource spent (from Lesson 1). Not models killed versus models lost; casualties are a cost paid, not the thing bought.

④ Why is time singled out as the resource you can't refund?

show answer

The battle lasts a fixed number of rounds, each spent whether you use it well or not, with no reserve to draw on. A wasted turn is spent as surely as a dead unit, but it rarely feels like a cost until the game ends a round short.

Primary source — read this one Read Sun Tzu's Chapter II, Waging War, in the Giles translation (Project Gutenberg #132) or the MIT Internet Classics Archive. It is short, and it is the oldest surviving statement of this exact principle: fighting is a cost, prolonged fighting bankrupts even the winner, and the best resource is the one you take from the enemy. Read it with the exchange-rate idea in mind and the parallels are hard to miss.
