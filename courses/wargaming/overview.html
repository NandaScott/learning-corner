Wargaming Fundamentals

Five principles that turn a wargame from a brawl into an optimisation problem — taught as a transferable strategic model, with Warhammer 40,000 as the concrete instantiation and Sun Tzu's Art of War as the 2,500-year-old proof that the principles were never about toy soldiers. Master the model; the specific tactics regenerate themselves.

Part 1 · The axiom

The one principle the other four depend on — it assigns value to every decision.

L1 Value flows from the win conditionMission score, not body count. Why "I killed more and lost" optimises the wrong metric; why denying a point equals scoring one; why a lead is never a stored balance. →

Part 2 · The four operating principles

Given the axiom, four levers move the score-difference. Each has a mechanism, a 40K instantiation, and a blunt heuristic.

L2 The economy of exchangesEvery action is a purchase; the only question is the exchange rate. Winning trades where your models die, opportunity cost, and overpaying while you win the fight. → L3 Threat range & the control of spaceMove + reach = the area you control whether you fire or not. Contested overlap, terrain that breaks symmetry, and reading one turn forward. → L4 Concentration of forceThe removal cliff — a unit does 100% of its job until it dies, then zero. Local superiority, the price of going thin, and sizing the commitment as a margin problem. → L5 Commitment under uncertaintyThe capstone: when to fire the conclusion. Deferral as a superposition of threats, tempo cost, reversibility, and tuning variance to the scoreboard. →

Part 3 · Putting it together

The principles are a connected model with a load-bearing order — plus the two habits that keep you honest.

L6 The five-step chainValue → cost → space → concentration → timing. Why the order is load-bearing, and why every specific tactic is just one of the five firing in a board state. → L7 Two cross-cutting skillsModel the opponent by capability, not psychology. Distrust the plan you can most easily justify — reasoning is a lawyer for what you already wanted. →

Part 4 · Review

Prove you can run all five cold — then run it again days later.
