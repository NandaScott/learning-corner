# Mission

**Own the fundamentals of wargaming as a transferable strategic model — the five principles, the chain that orders them, and the two cross-cutting skills — deeply enough to explain each one cold and reason about fresh situations, with Warhammer 40,000 as the concrete instantiation and Sun Tzu's *Art of War* as the timeless framing.**

## Why this mission

The learner already wrote the synthesis (`five-principles-of-wargaming.md`) — this course is not first-contact with the ideas; it is turning a rough, self-authored outline into a **durable, connected, retrieval-tested model**. The win condition of the *course* (chosen explicitly): **theory-first mastery, anchored to real 40K decisions.** Own the transferable strategic model — the kind that survives being ported to a different game or a non-game contest — but keep every principle nailed to a concrete board state so it never floats off into abstraction.

This is the same mould as the learner's other courses (connection-pooling, Celery, CSP): the goal is *storage strength*, not in-the-moment fluency. Explain the principle, the mechanism *behind* it (why nonlinear combat output makes concentration work; why turn-based play forces one-turn-forward reading), and the sharp edges — from memory, days later, unprompted.

## The three framings, and how they relate

- **The theory** (the spine): five principles + a load-bearing chain + two cross-cutting skills. This is what gets mastered.
- **Warhammer 40,000** (the instantiation, **11th edition**): the concrete game whose rules make the abstract principles *measurable* — VP, terrain objectives + Objective Control, command points, activations, wounds, threat range. 11th-edition scoring (win on the mission, controlled via OC on terrain, primary mission set by force dispositions) is Principle 1 written into the rulebook.
- **Sun Tzu's *Art of War*** (the framing): the 2,500-year-old statement that these principles are not about toy soldiers — they are the structure of contest itself. Each lesson closes the loop back to a verified Giles-translation passage.

## Grounding constraints

- **Source-first, never parametric.** Sun Tzu quotes are verified verbatim against the Lionel Giles translation (Project Gutenberg). 40K mechanics are verified against the current **11th-edition** core rules (the official free GW Core Rules / Wahapedia's 11th-ed mirror) — teach *the actual scoring model* (terrain objectives, OC, force dispositions), not folklore about "capturing objective markers."
- **Tool-specific truth over generic theory.** Where the general principle and 40K's specific implementation differ, flag it. (E.g. "denying a point = scoring one" is exactly true only because *the difference* of two totals decides — a rule fact, not a metaphor.)
- **Theory-first framing.** The learner knows the rules but plays rarely, so: assume the mechanics (CP, activations, wounds), but supply the concrete board examples rather than lean on their recent games.

## Starting level (from the mission questions)

**Wrote the outline; knows 40K rules; plays rarely.** So the course does not re-derive the ideas from scratch and does not explain what a command point is. It *sharpens* — pins the mechanism under each principle, verifies the Sun Tzu parallels, hardens the chain, and builds the retrieval scaffolding the raw outline lacks.

## What "success" looks like

The learner can, unprompted:
1. State **Principle 1** (value flows from the win condition) and explain why "I killed more and lost" is optimising the wrong metric — in 40K terms *and* as the Sun Tzu "win first, then fight" claim. *(L1)*
2. Run each of the **five principles** cold, with its mechanism, its 40K instantiation, its Sun Tzu parallel, and its blunt heuristic. *(L1–L5)*
3. Walk the **five-step chain** in order and explain why the order is load-bearing. *(L6)*
4. Apply the **two cross-cutting skills** — model the opponent by capability, distrust the plan you can most easily justify — to a fresh board state. *(L7)*
5. Survive the "why" follow-ups: why nonlinear output rewards removal over chipping; why deferral is a superposition of threats; why variance appetite flips with the scoreboard.

## Notes for the teacher

- The learner drives hard into mechanism and reasons about dependency order unprompted — Principle 1 is the axiom the other four depend on; teach it first and make the dependency explicit.
- The five principles are a *connected model*, not a list. Every specific tactic is one of the five firing in a board state — teach so the specifics become regenerable, not memorised.
- Prioritise storage strength: recall widgets per lesson, spacing across sessions, an interleaved quiz once there's enough material.
