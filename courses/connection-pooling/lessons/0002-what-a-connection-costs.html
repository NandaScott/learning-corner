Connection Pooling · Lesson 2

What a connection costs at scale

Lesson 1 showed the mechanism — the fork, the auth, the per-process cost. Now the consequence: add those costs up across thousands of requests and hundreds of backends, and you hit a wall pooling exists to keep you off.

The three costs, totalled up

From Lesson 1 we know each connection is a forked backend process that must handshake and authenticate. Tally what that means in aggregate — you should be able to rattle these off:

CostRoughlyWhy it exists
Setup latency~20–100 msthe Lesson 1 sequence: TCP round trip, fork, multi-message auth, session setup2
Idle memory~5–10 MB eacheach backend holds its own work memory + caches, even when doing nothing2
Concurrency draggrows with countthe context-switching + ProcArrayLock contention from Lesson 13
The math that makes it click 
max_connections = 500
idle cost       ≈ 7 MB × 500
              ≈ 3.5 GB of RAM gone
              before a single useful query runs

Idle connections are not free seats. They are paid-for, empty chairs.

The ceiling: max_connections

Postgres refuses to fork backends past max_connections (default 100). Hit the ceiling and the next client gets:

FATAL: sorry, too many clients already

The instinct is "just raise it." But each slot costs RAM and adds the concurrency drag from Lesson 1, so you can't raise it to infinity — and past a few hundred active backends, throughput goes down, not up. The CPU spends its time context-switching (cold caches, TLB flushes) and fighting over internal locks instead of running your queries.3

Interview line "A Postgres connection isn't free because it's a forked backend process with private memory. So connections are bounded by RAM and CPU, not sockets — and there's a hard max_connections ceiling you can't just raise away, because past the sweet spot more backends make the database slower."
The trap More connections ≠ more throughput. There's a sweet spot — often only a few times your core count of active connections; beyond it you're paying to make the database slower. This is exactly the curve pooling lets you stay on.

Why this is the setup for pooling

Put Lessons 1 and 2 together:

  • Opening a connection is expensive (Lesson 1: ~20–100 ms of fork + auth).
  • Keeping many open is expensive (RAM + context-switch drag).
  • And there's a hard ceiling you can't just raise away.

A naïve web app opens a fresh connection per request — paying the setup cost every time — and under load tries to open thousands at once, slamming into the ceiling. Pooling exists to break both: reuse a small set of already-open connections instead of forking a new backend per request. That's Lesson 3.

Recall — close your eyes and answer out loud before peeking:

① Name the three costs of a connection at scale.

show answer

Setup latency (~20–100 ms), idle memory (~5–10 MB each), and concurrency drag (context switches + lock contention) that grows with the number of backends.

② Why can't you fix "too many clients" by setting max_connections = 10000?

show answer

Each slot costs RAM and adds context-switch + lock contention. Past a few hundred active backends throughput drops — you'd be paying to make the DB slower, not faster.

③ In one sentence, what problem does pooling solve?

show answer

It reuses a small set of already-open backends instead of forking a new one per request — avoiding both the per-request setup cost and the connection-count ceiling.
